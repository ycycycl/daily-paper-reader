# MPCoT: Reward-Guided Multi-Path Latent Reasoning for Test-Time Scalable Vision-Language-Action 

Boyang Zhang 

Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering Boston University Boston, MA 02215, USA 

theostnc@bu.edu 

Lianlei Shan 

Department of Computer Science Tsinghua University Beijing 100084, China 

shanlianlei18@ucas.edu.cn 

Abstract: Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies remain brittle in long-horizon and high-uncertainty control, where one-pass action decoding provides limited inference-time deliberation. Explicit chain-of-thought can increase reasoning depth, but introduces token latency and an indirect text-to-action interface. We propose MPCoT , a reward-guided multi-path latent reasoning framework that initializes M hypotheses, refines them for K weight-tied steps, and softly ag-gregates them before action decoding. A training-only path-preference objec-tive evaluates candidate action branches with expert-action consistency, world-model/VLM-based progress, and success feedback to align the latent path scorer with downstream execution quality. MPCoT preserves the original 8-step action interface, generates zero reasoning tokens, and exposes configurable inference controls (K, M ). Under matched protocols on LIBERO and CALVIN, MPCoT improves long-horizon performance, with ablations confirming depth-width ef-fects, confidence-weighted aggregation, and reward-guided path supervision. 

Keywords: vision-language-action, latent reasoning, test-time scaling 

Code and videos: https://github.com/EDGSCOUT/MPCoT 

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies unify perception, language grounding, and action genera-tion for robot manipulation [1, 2, 3], while open frameworks such as OpenVLA and OpenVLA-OFT make this paradigm reproducible on benchmarks such as LIBERO [4, 5, 6]. Yet long-horizon exe-cution, compositional instructions, and uncertainty remain brittle: a fixed one-pass action decoder provides little inference-time deliberation, and small early errors can compound across later action chunks. This exposes a tension between reasoning depth and control efficiency. Explicit CoT can improve multi-step reasoning in language models and VLA control [7, 8], but textual or visual traces add latency, memory overhead, and an indirect interface to continuous actions. Purely implicit policies are efficient but often reduce difficult decisions to one-pass mappings. Here, we use “latent rea-soning” to mean iterative refinement and selection of continuous latent control hypotheses, rather than explicit symbolic reasoning or decoded rationales. Latent reasoning offers a middle ground: continuous CoT, latent compression, SoftCoT, and recurrent-depth VLA show that deliberation can occur without decoding intermediate text and can benefit robotic policies [9, 10, 11, 12]. This mo-tivates an internal VLA compute mechanism with depth/width controls and an unchanged action interface, making gains attributable to repeated correction, hypothesis coverage, or learned path selection rather than an opaque backbone change.

Contributions. The main contributions are fourfold: • We frame VLA reasoning as an embodied test-time compute allocation problem between high-cost explicit reasoning and shallow one-pass control. • We introduce MPCoT, a recurrent multi-path latent reasoning module with explicit depth/width controls (K, M ), zero reasoning tokens, and an unchanged action interface. • We introduce reward-guided path preference learning to align latent branch scores with downstream execution quality using action consistency, VLM progress, and success feed-back. • We validate MPCoT on LIBERO/CALVIN, showing benchmark gains, depth/width com-plementarity, efficient scaling, and reward-supervised path preference learning. 

## 2 RELATED WORK 

Open VLA policies and benchmarks. VLA research has progressed from language-grounded af-fordance selection and robot transformers to open VLA backbones and efficient fine-tuning frame-works [13, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. OpenVLA-OFT is our reference backbone because it provides a strong reproducible 8-step action-chunk interface. Recent methods such as π0, UnifiedVLA, FLOWER, VLA-Adapter, and AVA-VLA improve VLA policies through stronger action modeling, architec-ture design, or adaptation strategies [14, 15, 16, 17, 18]. MPCoT is orthogonal to these advances: it inserts lightweight latent deliberation before the unchanged action head and evaluates this internal compute mechanism on LIBERO and CALVIN, acting as an internal reasoning layer rather than a backbone replacement. 2Inputs

Reasoning-augmented VLA and latent test-time compute. Explicit reasoning methods such as CoT-VLA, TraceVLA, and WorldVLA make intermediate deliberation visible, but textual, visual, or trace-based steps can introduce token/intermediate-prediction overhead and do not always align nat-urally with continuous closed-loop control [8, 19, 20]. Continuous and compressed reasoning meth-ods instead show that deliberation can occur in hidden space without decoding intermediate text [9, 10, 21, 22, 23], and SoftCoT motivates soft hypothesis preservation and confidence-weighted selection under uncertainty [11]. Closest to MPCoT, recurrent-depth VLA studies how repeated latent updates improve a single refinement trajectory [12], while broader test-time-compute work analyzes accuracy–latency trade-offs [24]. MPCoT treats latent inference compute as depth–width allocation. Depth K repeatedly corrects each hypothesis with shared weights, whereas width M

maintains grounded latent hypotheses before one unchanged policy head. The scorer selects among execution-aware hypotheses, so width is not an ensemble, extra action heads, action-token expan-sion, inference-time rollout, or an external evaluator. Reward feedback trains only this scorer and is removed at inference. 

## 3 METHOD 

Overview. Fig. 2 details the MPCoT architecture and its training-time path supervision. At each control step, MPCoT initializes M latent hypotheses, applies K shared refinement steps, and ag-gregates the refined branches with confidence-aware soft weights before action decoding [11]. MPCoT is neither a multi-policy ensemble nor inference-time search: it runs one policy head with lightweight latent refinement. All intermediate reasoning remains in continuous latent space [9, 10, 21, 22, 23], avoiding rationale tokens. During training only, candidate branches receive reward feedback that supervises the scorer used later for reward-free inference. This modular place-ment attributes improvements to latent reasoning before the same action head, with inference over-head captured by reported latency rather than hidden in reward search. 

3.1 Problem Setup and Design Targets 

At each control step t, the policy receives observation ot and instruction l and predicts action chunk 

at. We apply a latent reasoning module Rθ before the policy decoder π0:

¯zt = Rθ (ot, l ; K, M ), at ∼ π0(a | ¯zt), (1) 

Rθ refines the same perception-language evidence before policy decoding. We instantiate π0 with OpenVLA-OFT [5] and enforce an unchanged action interface, explicit compute control, and small 3overhead through weight tying. Here K controls how many times the same recurrent refiner is applied, and M controls how many hypotheses are instantiated from the same context; (K, M ) = (0 , 1) recovers direct decoding and M = 1 reduces MPCoT to single-path latent iterative reasoning. This separation tests whether gains come from depth, width, or their combination before the same decoder.

3.6 Architecture and Complexity 

All quantitative results use fixed configurations to isolate latent depth and width; the adaptive budget controller is described in Appendix A and is not used in the reported numbers. Fθ is a lightweight weight-tied residual MLP, hω is a two-layer path scorer, and hypothesis codes are learned jointly. MPCoT adds about 2.7% parameters relative to the backbone, while inference cost scales approx-imately linearly with K × M because refinement weights are shared across steps. ## 4 EXPERIMENTS 

4.1 Experimental Setup, Metrics, and Protocol 

We insert MPCoT into OpenVLA-OFT and use official evaluation code and splits. Unless noted, training/evaluation follow OpenVLA-OFT defaults [5]; MPCoT preserves same 8-step action inter-face. Main results use fixed inference (K, M ) = (5 , 4) ; other settings appear in depth K/width M

ablations. They change latent steps and hypotheses only, not backbone, decoder, action horizon, splits, or observations. Training-side candidate evaluation supervises path preference, not train-to-test scheduling. Settings are in Appendix A. LIBERO is a near-ceiling compatibility benchmark, while CALVIN ABC →D and mechanism ablations provide primary evidence for sequential robust-ness, depth/width scaling, and reward-guided path supervision. 

Metrics. For LIBERO, we report suite-level and average success rates (SR). For CALVIN ABC →D, we report 1–5-step SR and average successful sequence length, reflecting error accumu-lation over longer instruction chains. For reward-supervision analysis, Path Consistency measures agreement between the scorer-preferred path and the highest-return path; Appendix B gives the definition. Mechanism ablations additionally report path similarity and stability.

We next isolate MPCoT’s structural components: Table 3 varies reasoning depth K and width M ,while Table A.3 studies aggregation and diversity. Table 3 shows complementary effects of depth and width. With M = 1 , increasing K from 1 to 5 improves LIBERO average/Long SR from 98.2/97.9 to 98.6/98.4, indicating that repeated latent correction improves a single control hypothesis. At fixed K = 3 , increasing M to 4 further improves Long SR to 98.6, suggesting that parallel paths provide alternative hypotheses. The best (K, M ) = (5 , 4) reaches 98.9 average SR, 98.9 Long SR, and 96.8 CALVIN 3-step SR. Appendix E further shows that soft aggregation with diversity regularization reduces path similarity from 0.91 to 0.66 and stability std from 0.41 to 0.18, indicating non-duplicated branches. 

4.5 Test-Time Scaling and Reasoning Efficiency 

Table 4 tests whether MPCoT scales test-time compute through latent depth and width while avoid-ing explicit-CoT token overhead. Comparing fixed inference configurations, (K, M ) = (5 , 4) improves LIBERO Long from 98.3 to 98.9 over (3 , 2) , while increasing measured latency from 31ms to 38ms and preserving zero reasoning-token overhead. The matched textual CoT baseline reaches 98.2 Long SR but incurs 110– 160ms latency, supporting the efficiency advantage of keeping deliberation in latent space. Thus MPCoT adds only the reported latent-refinement cost at inference, not a reward/world-model/VLM evaluation loop, while improving long-horizon control.

## 5 CONCLUSION 

We introduced MPCoT, a reward-guided multi-path latent reasoning module that supports VLA in-ference compute through repeated latent refinement depth K and active hypothesis width M while preserving the original action interface and generating zero reasoning tokens. On LIBERO and CALVIN, two standardized and widely used VLA benchmarks, MPCoT improves long-horizon per-formance over strong baselines; the ablations further support the roles of depth/width scaling, soft diverse aggregation, and training-only path preference learning. 

## 6 LIMITATIONS 

Our results are obtained on controlled benchmark environments, which are appropriate for isolating the latent reasoning mechanism and comparing against prior VLA methods under reproducible pro-tocols. However, benchmarks do not fully capture embodiment variation, sensing noise, hardware latency, and contact-rich failures in real robots. At the same time, isolated real-robot demonstrations can also be task- and platform-specific, and are not automatically more representative than standard-ized benchmarks. The next step is therefore controlled hardware validation that preserves the same protocol discipline while testing robustness under real deployment conditions. MPCoT also relies on a training-side world-model/VLM evaluator for path preference supervision, although this eval-uator is never queried at inference. Clarification of training-side and inference-side configurations. The training-side candidate-evaluation configuration and the inference reasoning configuration serve different purposes. The former controls how many candidate branches are used to compute auxiliary path-preference super-vision during optimization, while the latter controls how many latent refinement steps and active hypotheses are used by the deployed policy during evaluation. They are reported separately for reproducibility and should not be interpreted as a progressive train-to-test budget schedule. 

Training-side reward evaluation. The progress evaluator is an auxiliary world-model/VLM mod-ule used only during training. Given the current scene, instruction, and a candidate action branch, it predicts or scores the after-action scene and returns a scalar progress signal. Its role is to provide detached relative preference supervision for the path scorer; it is not the deployed policy, is not queried at inference, and is kept fixed across reward ablations. 

Optional adaptive budget rule. Although all quantitative results use fixed inference (K, M ) con-figurations, MPCoT can optionally allocate compute using previous-step uncertainty: 

Kt = min( Kmax , K min + ⌊βK ut−1⌋) , (A.1) 

Mt = min( Mmax , M min + ⌊βM ut−1⌋) , (A.2) where u0 is initialized by a default budget. This optional controller is a deployment extension and is not used in reported results.
