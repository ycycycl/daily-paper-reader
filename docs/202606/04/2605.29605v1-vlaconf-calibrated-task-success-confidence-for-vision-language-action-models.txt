# VLAConf: Calibrated Task-Success Confidence for Vision-Language-Action Models 

Dehao Huang 1,2, Aoxiang Gu 1, Chengjie Zhang 1, Bolin Zou 1,Wenlong Dong 1, Zilang Cen 2,3, Yue Wang 2, and Hong Zhang 1

Abstract — Confidence estimation for Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models is essential for robots to perform manipulation tasks in the open world, providing crucial signals for risk-sensitive decision-making and failure anticipation. Existing confidence estimation methods typically rely on ensemble-based paradigms or action-token probabilities to predict the likelihood of task success. However, they still encounter challenges in computational efficiency and cross-architecture generalizability. These methods usually require repeated sampling, leading to inference inefficiency, and are restricted to VLA models with discrete action outputs, making them difficult to apply to continuous action spaces. To address this issue, we propose VLAConf, a one-class discriminative confidence framework. By leveraging frozen pretrained VLA internal representations, VLAConf directly estimates step-wise anomaly scores in a single forward pass using a lightweight confidence head, thereby eliminating the overhead of exhaustive resampling. We ad-ditionally use step-conditioned modeling to encode rollout-phase information along the manipulation trajectory. Experi-ments on the LIBERO benchmark demonstrate that VLAConf significantly improves the quality of the confidence signal constructed for post-hoc calibration, outperforming existing baselines by a large margin in inference efficiency. The effective-ness of VLAConf is further validated in real-robot experiments. To access the source code and supplementary videos, visit https://sites.google.com/view/vlaconf. 

I. INTRODUCTION Confidence estimation is essential for deploying Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models in open-world robotic ma-nipulation, as illustrated in Figure 1. Although VLA models have shown strong cross-task generalization, real-world ex-ecution still involves distribution shifts, long-tail scenarios, and unexpected failures. Therefore, a robot should not only generate control actions, but also predict how likely it is to successfully complete the current task. Such confidence supports risk-sensitive decision-making, failure anticipation, action rollback, and human-robot collaboration. Motivated by the need for a reliable estimate of task success itself, we study how to derive a success-relevant signal from pretrained VLA representations and calibrate it into task-success confidence for reliable evaluation. Existing VLA confidence methods predominantly follow an ensemble-based paradigm, where confidence is derived from predictive disagreement across multiple models or prompts and then calibrated post hoc. A. Confidence Estimation and Uncertainty in VLAs 

Recent vision-language-action (VLA) models have demonstrated strong semantic grounding and broad manipulation capabilities by combining pretrained vision-language backbones with action generation modules [1]–[5]. Despite this progress, evaluating the reliability of VLA systems during deployment remains challenging. In the broader context of large language and vision-language models, uncertainty quantification often relies on token-level probabilities, semantic entropy, or generation consistency [6]–[10]. Inspired by these approaches, initial efforts toward con-fidence estimation for VLAs have predominantly followed an ensemble-based paradigm [11]–[13]. Notably, Confi-denceVLA [14] shares our goal of providing calibrated task-success confidence for VLA models. They achieve this by deriving confidence through predictive disagreement across prompt ensembles and action-token probabilities. While con-ceptually appealing, this strategy faces two critical limita-tions in modern robotic deployment. First, repeated sampling and ensemble inference are computationally expensive, mak-ing them difficult to reconcile with the high-frequency, real-time control requirements of physical robots. Second, rely-ing on action-token probabilities restricts these methods to discrete-action VLAs. For state-of-the-art VLAs that output continuous actions (e.g., via diffusion or flow-matching [1]), disagreement-based uncertainty is often unstable and difficult to interpret directly as a success likelihood. To achieve an efficient, continuous-action-compatible confidence signal, we must shift our focus from the action-output space to the model’s internal representation space. 

B. Out-of-Distribution and Failure Detection in Robotics 

Extracting reliability signals from internal representations is deeply connected to out-of-distribution (OOD) and failure detection in robot manipulation. A practical regime assumes that failures are scarce, expensive, or unsafe to collect, meaning successful executions provide the only reliable supervision. Traditional OOD methods—such as density estimation, one-class discriminators, distance metrics, and consistency checks [15]–[21]—excel in this regime because they are strictly success-only . Recent works, such as FAIL-Detect [16], systematically evaluate these methods for gener-ative imitation learning policies. We share this success-only philosophy, but these methods have largely been designed for task-specific visuomotor policies. Scaling them to gener-alist VLAs introduces a significant multi-task generalization challenge, as the diverse visual scenes and novel language instructions inherent to generalist deployment can easily be misclassified as OOD anomalies. To handle the complexity of generalist policies, a separate line of work explores supervised failure detection [22]–[25]. For instance, SAFE [26] demonstrates that the internal latent features of VLAs capture rich, task-agnostic semantics about execution success, and successfully trains a multi-task failure classifier on top of these representations. While this resolves the multi-task generalization issue, it fundamentally relies on collecting diverse failed rollouts for supervised training, which violates the practical success-only constraint and poses significant safety risks in real-world open-vocabulary manipulation. 

Positioning of Our Approach. Our work, VLAConf, synthesizes these perspectives to provide a practical confi-dence signal for modern VLAs. Like ConfidenceVLA [14], our primary objective is to produce calibrated task-success confidence. However, we achieve this in a single forward pass without relying on discrete action tokens or expensive ensembles. To do so, we adopt the success-only philosophy of traditional OOD methods [16] by formulating confidence estimation as a one-class discriminative problem. However, we apply it directly to the frozen, rich latent representations of VLAs—similar to supervised methods like SAFE [26]—to ensure multi-task generalization. By training a lightweight confidence head with a Coin-Flip Network objective on successful rollouts alone, VLAConf bridges these paradigms, delivering an efficient, backbone-agnostic confidence mea-sure naturally suited for continuous-action generalist policies. A. Overview 

An overview of VLAConf is shown in Figure 3. Given a rollout prefix ρt = ( o1, . . . , o t) and instruction l, VLAConf estimates task-success confidence by measuring whether the observed execution remains close to the success manifold represented by Dsft . The method first extracts a step-wise execution representation from a frozen VLA, maps each step to an anomaly score —following standard one-class OOD detection terminology, a non-negative scalar that grows when the input falls outside the support of successful executions— with a step-conditioned scoring head, and finally aggregates and calibrates these scores into p(Y = 1 | ρt, l ).We first describe how VLAConf constructs backbone-agnostic execution features from frozen VLA hidden states. We then introduce the step-conditioned anomaly scoring objective trained only on successful trajectories. Finally, we present prefix-level aggregation and post-hoc calibration, which convert step-wise anomaly scores into calibrated task-success probabilities. 

B. Frozen VLA Execution Representation 

The first step is to obtain a representation on which support can be estimated consistently across VLA architectures. We estimate confidence in the internal representation space of a pretrained VLA rather than in its action-output space. This is important because manipulation failures are frequently reflected by whether the current multimodal context departs from familiar successful behavior, not merely by whether the immediate action prediction appears sharp. Moreover, action probabilities are often unavailable or hard to compare across architectures, especially when the action head is continuous rather than token based. Let fθ denote a pretrained VLA backbone. We keep fθ

frozen throughout confidence learning. This choice preserves the semantic and geometric priors already learned by the VLA and keeps confidence estimation efficient because only a small head is optimized. Thus, VLAConf treats the pretrained VLA as a general-purpose multimodal feature extractor and learns confidence on top of that representation rather than by adapting the policy itself. At step k, the backbone processes the current observation-instruction pair (ok, l ) and returns hidden states Hk. Al-though different backbones expose these hidden states through different tensor layouts, VLAConf only assumes a shared pooled-feature interface. We separately pool the valid visual tokens and language tokens, yielding a visual summary 

hvk and a language summary hlk. These summaries capture the scene content and task semantics needed for confidence estimation. We additionally fuse the robot proprioceptive state because visual-language context alone may not fully determine whether the current execution phase is healthy. Variables such as gripper opening and end-effector pose often distinguish between a normal state and an abnormal one even when the image appears similar. We project the proprioceptive component xk with a small state encoder and fuse it with the pooled visual-language features: 

Hk = fθ (ok, l ),hvk = Pool v (Hk), hlk = Pool l(Hk),hxk = Ex(xk), zk = Mmix ([ hvk; hlk; hxk ]) ,

where Pool v (·) and Pool l(·) denote masked mean pooling over the valid visual and language token positions, Ex(·) is a small MLP state encoder, and Mmix (·) is a two-layer mixing MLP. The result is a compact execution representation zk

on which confidence is learned. This representation hides backbone-specific implementation details while retaining the visual, semantic, and proprioceptive information needed to assess familiarity. C. Step-Conditioned Anomaly Scoring 

Given the execution feature zk, we next learn a scalar anomaly score without requiring failure labels. We build this score on the Coin-Flip Network (CFN) of Lobel et al. [27], which fits a small head q(·) to predict an i.i.d. Rademacher target c ∼ {− 1, +1 }d for every input. Under the squared-error optimum, the network output for an input 

z encountered N (z) times collapses to the empirical mean of its targets, meaning its expected squared norm is proportional to 1/N (z). The squared norm is therefore a smooth anomaly score: features common in the training distribution yield small norms, while atypical features yield large ones. This makes CFN a natural fit for our success-only setting. Importantly, manipulation confidence is inherently phase-dependent: the same scene may correspond to normal behav-ior at one stage of a task and abnormal behavior at another (e.g., an open gripper is expected before grasp but suspicious after lift). Therefore, we condition the anomaly scoring head on the current rollout step, allowing the model to learn phase-specific notions of familiarity. To encode task phase, we introduce a step encoder that maps the rollout step index k to a compact descriptor. Let K be a fixed normalization horizon and let ˆk =clip( k−1, 0, K −1) . The descriptor combines a learned step embedding with a normalized progress scalar: 

ψk = [ ek; ¯k ], ek = Emb( ˆk), ¯k = ˆk/ (K−1) ,

where Emb( ·) is a learned embedding table of size K. Using a fixed K rather than the per-trajectory length Ti keeps ¯k

well-defined in the online setting, where the eventual rollout length is unknown; the clip ensures both ek and ¯k stay in range when k exceeds K−1 at inference. The resulting descriptor captures both discrete phase identity and coarse relative progress through the rollout. Instead of concatenating ψk with zk, we use the descriptor to modulate the anomaly scoring head with a FiLM-style affine transform [28] followed by a residual gate, allowing the same execution representation to be interpreted differ-ently at different phases of the task. D. Prefix Aggregation and Calibration 

The step-wise anomaly score sk is local and uncalibrated, whereas the target quantity is rollout-level success proba-bility. A robot may briefly visit an unusual state and still recover, or it may appear normal early in the rollout and fail only after a later deviation. Confidence should therefore summarize the evidence accumulated across the observed prefix rather than depend on a single step in isolation. We therefore define a trajectory-level aggregated signal 

ut = ϕ(s1, . . . , s t), where ϕ(·) aggregates the step-wise scores up to time t. The most suitable aggregation rule depends on the deployment protocol. In the pre-execution setting, only the initial observation is available, so we set 

u1 = s1. In the online setting, we consider simple aggre-gation rules such as the running mean, ut = 1

> t

Ptk=1 sk, or the maximum prefix score. This modularity allows the same step-wise model to support both one-shot risk assessment before execution and continuous confidence tracking during rollout. The aggregated signal ut is an uncalibrated scalar. To con-vert it into an interpretable success probability, we perform post-hoc calibration on a small set of outcome-labeled policy rollouts, Dcal (as introduced in Section III). This is the only stage of VLAConf that consumes outcome labels, requiring only a binary outcome Y ∈ { 0, 1} per rollout rather than dense per-step annotations. We fit a standard Platt scaling model [29] to map the aggregated signal to a success probability: 

h(ut) = sigmoid( −α u t + β),

where α ≥ 0 and β ∈ R are learned by minimizing the binary cross-entropy on Dcal . The explicit minus sign encodes the prior that larger anomaly scores indicate lower probability of success. Overall, the pipeline adds only one MLP head and one scalar logistic regression per step, with negligible overhead relative to the VLA backbone and no additional sampling. V. E XPERIMENTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

Backbones. We evaluate VLAConf on two representative VLA backbones spanning the discrete- and continuous-action regimes: (i) O PEN VLA-OFT [1], a token-based VLA that produces actions through a discrete autoregressive head; and (ii) π0.5 [2], a state-of-the-art generalist VLA that emits con-tinuous actions via flow-matching. Both backbones are kept frozen during confidence learning. This pair lets us examine whether the same confidence signal can be constructed across heterogeneous action heads, and is also the setting under which prior action-token-based confidence methods break down. 

Benchmarks. We use the LIBERO benchmark suite [30] for in-distribution evaluation, covering three subsets that stress different aspects of manipulation: LIBERO-G OAL ,LIBERO-S PATIAL , and LIBERO-O BJECT . For robust-ness under shifted conditions, we additionally evaluate on LIBERO-P RO [31] and LIBERO-P LUS [32], which extend LIBERO with perturbations and previously unseen tasks (Section V-C). Real-robot results are reported in Section V-D. 

Baselines. We compare against three external reference points and one ablation. (i) PCA-KMEANS : a post-hoc clus-tering OOD baseline adapted from robot execution failure detection [33]. It projects frozen VLA features with PCA, clusters successful demonstration features with K-means, and uses the distance to the nearest centroid as an anomaly score. (ii) T OKEN PROB : the policy’s own action-token probability, aggregated over the action sequence. This lower-cost ref-erence re-uses information already produced by the VLA. (iii) C ONFIDENCE VLA [14]: a prompt-ensemble confidence method that derives task-success probability from action-token agreement across resampled prompts. TokenProb and ConfidenceVLA rely on per-token action probabilities and are therefore restricted to VLAs with discrete action heads; they are not directly applicable to continuous-action back-bones such as π0.5, and the corresponding entries are marked “/” in Table I. (iv) VLAC ONF -N OSTEP : an ablation of VLAConf that directly transfers prior one-class discrim-inator ideas, such as RND and CFM-style success-only scoring [34], [35], to frozen VLA representations. It uses the same frozen VLA features and one-class scoring objective as VLAConf, but removes the step encoder, isolating the effect of phase conditioning. 

Evaluation protocol. For each deployed policy, we reserve a small validation set of completed rollouts as Dcal , used to fit the two Platt-scaling parameters. We then evaluate on disjoint held-out rollouts and record, per rollout, (a) the binary outcome Y ∈ { 0, 1}, (b) the rollout length, and (c) confidence queries from each method. Confidence is queried in two regimes: PRE -EXECUTION , where only the initial observation o1 is available, and ONLINE EXECUTION , where the prefix ρt accumulates over the rollout and the available step-wise scores are aggregated (Section IV). For scalar online-execution table entries, we report the fixed checkpoint at 50% of policy execution (using max aggregation), so the comparison reflects an in-progress estimate available during deployment. 

Metrics. We report three calibration metrics that are standard for binary outcomes and capture complementary aspects of probability quality: E XPECTED CALIBRATION ERROR 

(ECE) measures the average gap between predicted confi-dence and empirical accuracy across confidence bins; B RIER SCORE measures the mean squared error between predicted probability and the binary outcome; and NEGATIVE LOG -

Implementation details. The CFN head is a two-layer MLP applied on top of frozen VLA features, with masked-mean pooling over visual and language tokens and a small MLP encoder for proprioceptive state. The step descriptor consists of a learned embedding Emb( ˆk) together with a normalized progress scalar ˆk/ (K−1) , where ˆk = clip( k−1, 0, K −1) 

and K is a fixed hyperparameter ( K=96 for π0.5, K=256 

for OpenVLA-OFT). The descriptor modulates the coin-scoring head through a FiLM affine transform with a post-affine LayerNorm and a sigmoid residual gate. Coin-vector dimension is d = 64 . We train the head with AdamW for the same number of optimization steps across backbones and suites; the only stage that consumes outcome labels is the per-suite Platt scaling. Unless otherwise stated, temporal analyses use the running mean of step-wise scores, while the main scalar online results use max aggregation as specified above.

Compared with VLAConf-NoStep, it also gives lower Brier score and NLL in both regimes, showing that action-agnostic applicability alone is not enough; the representation-level score still ben-efits from phase conditioning. More broadly, because VLA-Conf only assumes access to frozen internal representations rather than a particular action parameterization, the same design can naturally extend to recent World Action Model policies that expose visual or dynamics features for control and planning [36], [37].

C. Robustness under Shifted LIBERO Suites 

The standard LIBERO results establish the behavior of VLAConf under the original benchmark distribution. We next evaluate on LIBERO-Pro and LIBERO-Plus to test whether the confidence signal remains useful under system-atic distribution shifts, including task, object, layout, camera, robot-state, lighting, background, and sensor perturbations. These tasks cover rigid, articulated, and deformable manipulation, providing a deployment-facing complement to the benchmark studies above. Table III summarizes task-success confidence over 151 physical rollouts. VLAConf remains effective in the real-world setting, with average ECE, Brier score, and NLL of 0.1041 , 0.1595 , and 0.4885 across the three tasks. We propose VLAConf, a representation-level confidence framework for Vision-Language-Action models that derives calibrated task-success confidence from frozen internal fea-tures. Compared with existing confidence methods based on action-token probabilities or repeated sampling, VLAConf estimates confidence in a single forward pass and applies nat-urally to both discrete- and continuous-action backbones. Ex-periments on standard LIBERO suites and shifted LIBERO-Pro and LIBERO-Plus benchmarks demonstrate that VLA-Conf provides more informative task-success probabilities while remaining efficient, and that online evidence can further improve confidence quality during execution.
