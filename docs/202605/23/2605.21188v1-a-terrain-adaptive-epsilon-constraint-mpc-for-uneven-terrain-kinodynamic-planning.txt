# A Terrain-Adaptive ϵ-Constraint MPC for Uneven Terrain Kinodynamic Planning 

Otobong Jerome 1, Geesara Kalathunga 2, and Tiago Nascimento 1

Abstract —Kinodynamic planning for car-like vehicles on un-even terrain requires simultaneously optimizing competing ob-jectives such as path efficiency and pose stability. This work presents an adaptive ϵ-constraint method integrated into a Model Predictive Control (MPC) framework, where the ϵ bounds are dynamically adjusted based on terrain descriptors to explore the Pareto front in real time. To capture vehicle-terrain dynamics, we develop a semi-parametric model combining analytical vehicle dynamics with a Sparse Gaussian Process (SGP) trained on the same terrain descriptors. The proposed ϵ-MPC is evaluated against MPPI and GAKD baselines, achieving a 94% navigation success rate while reducing maximum orientation deviation by 

24% and improving multi-objective trade-off quality by 23% .

Index Terms —Constrained Motion Planning; Motion and Path Planning; Integrated Planning and Control 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# VARIOUS methods have been proposed for generating triangular meshes from sensor data [1], [2], including applications in large-scale environments. Robotics frameworks such as Move Base Flex (MBF) [3] offer adaptable tools for creating so-called Navigation Meshes and enabling robot navigation within these environments. However, navigating through complex terrains still poses significant challenges for autonomous robots, especially in unstructured environments such as outdoor construction sites, disaster zones, or planetary exploration. Robust navigation in these settings requires plan-ners that not only compute collision-free paths but also respect the kinodynamic constraints imposed by the robot’s dynam-ics, including velocity, acceleration limits however accurate vehicle-terrain modelling is challenging due to the effects of the terrain. Traditional mesh planners, such as the continuous vector planner (CVP) [4], effectively leverage the Fast Marching Method (FMM) [5] on multilayer 3D triangular meshes to generate smooth geodesic paths over rough terrains. These planners provide a global, continuous shortest-path vector field that encapsulates detailed terrain attributes such as roughness and elevation. Despite their success in producing terrain-aware geometric paths, they do not explicitly incorporate kin-odynamic constraints, limiting their applicability to scenarios where dynamic feasibility is critical. Kinodynamic planning addresses motion planning prob-lems under both kinematic and dynamic constraints. The first

Earlier work also explored solutions using random-ized approaches, variants of RRT and RRT, which incorporate kinodynamic cost optimization [7], [8]. Recent kinodynamic planning research falls into two main categories: non-data-driven and data-driven approaches. Gradient-free methods like Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) control are favoured in the former for managing complex dynamics without explicit gradients. Data-driven methods use models such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to learn system dynamics and optimize planning in high-dimensional or partially observed settings. Recent advances in DRL have demonstrated potential for learning complex navigation policies in dynamic environments [9], [10], yet several critical limitations persist. Most existing approaches are confined to 2D planning and sparse reward functions, with poor generalization across diverse start-goal configurations. Furthermore, current DRL-based planners lack integration with continuous global guidance structures and do not account for kinodynamic constraints, particularly on uneven terrain. Standard RL reward formulations also fail to capture the multi-objective nature of kinodynamic planning, which can be solved using an ϵ-constraint methods, where path optimality and physical stability must be jointly considered. Although, ϵ-constraint methods have been extensively applied in multi-objective optimization research, their use in uneven terrain kinodynamic planning remains under-explored in the existing literature. Classic discrete geodesic methods include the MMP [11] and CH [12] algorithms (and their variants [13], [14]), known for precision on smaller meshes, alongside the fast marching method (FMM) [5] and heat method [15], which scale better to larger datasets. The more recent edge-flip method [16] refines geodesic paths via local mesh modifications, though

Kinodynamic planning on such meshes then requires balancing proximity to the optimal geodesic against the robot’s kinematic and dynamic constraints. Gradient-based kinodynamic planning methods including Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) [19], Model Predic-tive Control (MPC) [20], Covariant Hamiltonian Optimization for Motion Planning (CHOMP) [21], [22], and Iterative Lin-ear Quadratic Regulator (ILQR) [23]offer strong convergence properties but face limitations such as sensitivity to initial conditions, local optima trapping, and high online computa-tion costs, particularly in non-linear and non-convex settings. Gradient-free alternatives like Model Predictive Path Integral Control (MPPI) [24] and Genetic Algorithmic Kinodynamic (GAKD) planning [25] circumvent these issues by avoiding gradient computations entirely: MPPI evaluates multiple in-dependent control sequences in parallel for broad solution space coverage, while GAKD extends this by replacing cost-weighted trajectory averaging with a genetic algorithm that selects and recombines the best trajectory segments [25], en-abling robust real-time performance in dynamically challeng-ing environments. However, these methods rely on accurate dynamics models that can be difficult to derive analytically for car-like vehicles on arbitrary 3D mesh terrain; to this end, one contributions of this paper is to provide a semi-parametric approach blending data-driven flexibility with physical struc-ture. Moreover, in MPPI and GAKD, weighted scalarization typically allows the path length objective to dominate the solution, so we propose a cost formulation that prevents this dominance and yields a balanced multi-objective outcome. Reinforcement Learning (RL) has shown promise for mobile robot navigation but faces challenges including sparse rewards, slow convergence, poor generalizability, and limited inter-pretability. Various hybrid strategies address these limitations, hierarchical architectures combining global planners with local RL agents [26], [27], sensor-based feedback and transfer learning [28], [29], modular designs decoupling deep learning components [30], [31], and attention mechanisms for improved interpretability [9], with a comprehensive review available in [32]. However, these approaches are predominantly designed for 2D planar environments or rely on elevation maps that do not fully capture the intricacies of uneven 3D mesh terrain. Hence, there is a need for a planner that explicitly addresses the inherent multi-objective nature of kinodynamic planning, balancing path optimality against physical stability, while generalizing to unseen uneven terrain configurations. The ϵ-constraint method is a classical technique in multi-objective optimization that converts a multi-objective problem into a series of single-objective sub-problems by constraining all but one objective within specified bounds. For instance, [33] and [34] explored the use of the ϵ-constraint method in intelligent parking lot power management and engineering optimization problems. While effective at generating Pareto-optimal solutions, the method’s computational cost limits its practicality in high-dimensional or real-time settings. In this work, this limitations is circumvented by sampling from the Pareto front, and applying the method within a receding horizon, also, the stability constraint is adaptively tightened or relaxed based on the local terrain conditions. A scaled continuous vector field is leveraged to provide global guidance, enabling consistent performance across diverse mesh environments. The proposed terrain-adaptive epsilon-constraint method systematically explore the trade-off frontier between competing objectives. The proposed semi-parametric vehicle-terrain dynamics model, enables offline development and generalization for online deployment. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

The uneven terrain planning is formulated as a receding horizon kinodynamic problem, seeking dynamically feasible trajectories that optimize multiple objectives while using the FMM vector field for global guidance. The problem is solved as an epsilon-constraint optimization: at each planning iter-ation, forward trajectories are simulated, and the sequence minimizing a progress objective is selected subject to aPREPRINT VERSION. 3

hard constraint whose tolerance is dynamically adjusted to local terrain characteristics. Candidate sequences are produced through warm-starting and Fast Marching Method (FMM)-biased sampling, by which exploration is guided toward promising regions of the environment. Along each trajectory, terrain descriptors are extracted and orientation deviations from a terrain-aligned reference frame are computed, yielding a physically meaningful stability risk measure. The resulting receding-horizon controller adapts both its constraint threshold and sampling strategy to the terrain being traversed, ensur-ing less computation on benign terrain while conservative behaviour is automatically enforced in challenging conditions.

The Fast Marching Method (FMM) [5], as employed by [4], computes a continuous vector field over a triangle mesh in O(n log n) time, where n is the number of passable vertices, with the field accessible at arbitrary points via barycentric interpolation: given direction vectors dA, dB , dC at triangle vertices A, B, C and barycentric coordinates (λ1, λ 2, λ 3) of a query point p ∈ △ABC , the interpolated direction is

We evaluate the proposed terrain-adaptive kinodynamic planning approach through comprehensive simulation exper-iments designed to assess navigation performance across diverse terrain conditions. The proposed evaluation focuses on three key research questions: (1) Does the FMM-biased sampling strategy improve trajectory quality compared to pure random sampling? (2) How does the terrain-adaptive epsilon constraint affect stability and path efficiency across varying terrain difficulties? (3) Can the planner handle dynamic reced-ing horizon replanning in real-time while maintaining safety? 

A. Experimental Setup 

Experiments are conducted using ROS 2 Humble with Gazebo simulator on two 3D mesh terrains: Mesh 1 (270,904 vertices, 536,879 faces) and Mesh 2 (59,107 vertices, 117,806 faces). Fifty start-end positions are chosen per mesh, classified as rough terrain or gentle slope scenarios, with each trial run 5 times due to stochastic sampling. We simulate an AgileX Scout Mini robot using the model in Section III-B with

C. Sensitivity Analysis 

Horizon Length. Table II reveals that longer planning hori-zons consistently improve performance but with diminishing returns. Success rate increases from 82% (5 steps) to 95% (25 steps), while trade-off metric T decreases from 0.72 to 0.59. However, planning time scales superlinearly, growing from 12ms to 56ms. The results suggest that a horizon of 20-25 steps provides an effective balance for real-time receding horizon planning in dynamic outdoor environments. 

Candidate Sample Size. Table III shows that trajectory quality improves monotonically with sample size. Increasing 

Ncand from 50 to 120 raises success rate from 78% to 95% and reduces trade-off metric T from 0.76 to 0.51. Planning time grows approximately linearly from 18ms to 38ms. The marginal improvement beyond Ncand = 100 suggests this represents a practical operating point, achieving 92% success rate with 32ms planning time. 

Adaptation Parameters. Table IV demonstrates that ter-rain adaptation strength significantly impacts the stability-efficiency trade-off. Higher values of γr and γs progressively reduce maximum orientation deviation from 31.5° to 14.5° while increasing path length deviation from 7.2m to 10.8m. The configuration γr = 0.6, γs = 0.7 achieves the best trade-off metric ( T = 0 .50 ) with 96% success rate, balancing aggressive stability constraints with path efficiency. Excessive conservatism ( γr = γs = 0 .8) yields perfect success but at the cost of 27% longer paths.

The real-world result is close to simulation trends: success rates due to unmodeled physical effects like wheel slip and sensor noise, while path deviations increase 22% as the planner selects more conservative routes under uncertainty. Real-world planning times are 15% slower but still meet the 100 ms real-time requirement. The proposed method’s performance advantages over baselines persist in physical deployment, validating that terrain-adaptive constraints scale appropriately to real-world conditions.

F. Failure Analysis 

Failed trials exhibited three modes: infeasible scenarios where goals lie in FMM-unreachable regions, local minima trapping in narrow corridors (mitigable by increasing Ncand 

to 100), and stability constraint violations at terrain discon-tinuities where descriptor extraction incompletely captures local geometry. We also explored NSGA-II multi-objective optimization to jointly optimize path efficiency and stability, but its population-based evolutionary search incurred planning times exceeding 2000 ms per iteration, over an order of mag-nitude slower than MPPI (29 ms) and GAKD (35 ms), while yielding comparable or worse success rates, confirming that for real-time receding horizon planning, simpler sampling-based methods with direct FMM guidance offer superior efficiency without sacrificing solution quality. 

G. Conclusion and Future Work 

The results demonstrate that while weighted scalarization methods can optimize individual objectives effectively, the PREPRINT VERSION. 8

proposed terrain-adaptive ϵ-constraint approach more effec-tively balances competing objectives by dynamically adjusting stability constraints based on local terrain features, enabling safer navigation without excessive path deviation. The modest computational overhead is justified by substantial improve-ments in feasibility and completion rate, particularly critical for autonomous systems in unstructured outdoor environments. The presented approach provides a principled framework for terrain-adaptive kinodynamic planning that balances global optimality, local safety, and computational tractability, with several promising directions for future work. Extending the framework to dynamic environments represents a natural next step, where integrating temporal prediction into FMM compu-tation and stability assessment would enable robust navigation alongside moving obstacles. Finally, expanding the terrain descriptor with additional semantic properties such as surface softness offers an exciting opportunity to refine traversability assessment and broaden the range of deployable environments.
