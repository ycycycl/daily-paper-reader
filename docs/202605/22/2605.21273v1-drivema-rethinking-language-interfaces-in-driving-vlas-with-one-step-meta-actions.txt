# DriveMA: Rethinking Language Interfaces in Driving VLAs with One-Step Meta-Actions

> 1

Abstract: Driving Vision-Language-Action Models (Driving VLAs) commonly introduce natural-language reasoning as an intermediate interface for end-to-end planning, but reasoning-centric interfaces face three practical bottlenecks: obtain-ing high-quality reasoning annotations is difficult, generating and understanding long reasoning chains is challenging for compact models, and inference latency is substantially increased. In this paper, we rethink the design of language inter-faces in Driving VLAs and show that concise one-step meta-actions are a sim-ple yet effective alternative to verbose reasoning. Meta-actions provide semantic decision grounding while remaining low-entropy, and being automatically deriv-able from expert trajectories, enabling scalable supervision and reliable trajectory conditioning. Building on this interface, we propose DriveMA, which combines action-centric supervised training with a turn-level credit-assignment reinforce-ment learning framework that jointly optimizes meta-action correctness, trajectory quality, and trajectory–meta-action consistency. Experiments show that DriveMA already achieves a new state of the art on the Waymo End-to-End Driving Chal-lenge with a 2B model, reaching a Rater Feedback Score (RFS) of 8.060 , while its 4B version further improves the state of the art to 8.079 ; DriveMA also obtains competitive performance on NAVSIM. Ablations demonstrate that one-step meta-actions offer a better practical trade-off between expressiveness, predictability, and inference efficiency than natural-language reasoning or finer-grained action sequences. Code, data, and models will be released to facilitate future research. 

Keywords: Autonomous Driving, Vision-Language-Action, Meta-Action 

1 Introduction 

Recent Driving Vision-Language-Action Models (Driving VLAs) introduce language as an inter-mediate interface between visual inputs and action outputs, enabling explicit scene understanding, traffic reasoning, and decision grounding for end-to-end planning [1, 2]. Existing methods often use open-ended natural-language reasoning or structured reasoning stages [3] such as perception, reasoning, and decision making, and have shown advantages over direct trajectory prediction [4, 5]. However, effective reasoning does not necessarily provide an efficient language interface, as long-chain natural-language reasoning introduces bottlenecks from both the data and model perspec-tives. First, high-quality reasoning annotations are expensive to obtain: human annotation is dif-ficult to scale, while rationales generated by stronger teacher models are hard to automatically verify for correctness and consistency. Second, compact Driving VLAs may struggle to generate low-hallucination reasoning and extract trajectory-relevant decisions from lengthy language con-texts [3, 6]. Moreover, generating lengthy reasoning chains also increases inference latency, making it less suitable for real-time planning [7, 4]. Beyond these reasoning-side bottlenecks, Driving VLAs also suffer from a persistent language-action gap: even a correct high-level language decision may not be faithfully executed by the predicted trajectory [8, 9]. On the other hand, removing language entirely sacrifices semantic decision grounding and may encourage shortcut learning from historical motion states.

As illustrated in Fig. 1, meta-action differs from both trajectory-only prediction and reasoning-centric interfaces: it remains in the language domain, but compresses high-dimensional future motion into a short, low-entropy driving intention rather than verbose natural-language reasoning [10]. This makes it easier to predict as an intermedi-ate step, following the coarse-to-fine logic of first resolving a semantic decision and then realizing it as continuous trajectories. Moreover, meta-actions can be automatically generated via trajectory clustering and rule-based mapping, making them scalable, verifiable, and directly supervisable. Despite being a natural candidate interface, meta-action still faces two key challenges. First, what temporal granularity should it adopt? Multi-step meta-actions provide richer future motion de-scriptions, but they enlarge the decision space and amplify error propagation. Second, introducing meta-actions alone does not close the language-action gap, since the predicted trajectory may still be inconsistent with the predicted high-level decision. To address these challenges, we propose 

DriveMA , a Driving VLA framework built on one-step meta-actions. We systematically compare meta-action granularities under both oracle and predicted settings, and find that one-step meta-action achieves the best practical expressiveness–reliability trade-off. Building on the verifiability of meta-actions, we further introduce a turn-level credit-assignment reinforcement learning (RL) framework. It provides precise turn-level credit assignment and dense rewards for meta-action prediction and tra-jectory generation, while introducing an explicit consistency reward that maps predicted trajectories back to meta-actions to align language decisions with low-level planning. Experiments show that one-step meta-action is a simple yet powerful language interface for Driving VLAs. Our method achieves a new state of the art on the Waymo Open Dataset Vision-based End-to-End Driving (WOD-E2E) [11], reaching a Rater Feedback Score (RFS) of 8.079 , while obtaining competitive results on NAVSIM [12] and significantly outperforming reasoning-centric Driving VLAs. Ablations and oracle analyses further show that although natural-language reasoning has higher information capacity, its practical utility is limited by the model’s ability to generate and exploit long reasoning chains. In contrast, one-step meta-action exposes the most trajectory-relevant decision with better learnability and planning utility. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: • We revisit the design of language interfaces in Driving VLAs and propose one-step meta-action as a simple, effective, and verifiable language-domain decision interface. • We systematically study meta-action granularity, which shows that one-step meta-action achieves the best balance between expressiveness and prediction reliability. • We introduce a turn-level credit-assignment RL framework. By exploiting the verifiability of meta-actions, the framework constructs a trajectory–meta-action consistency reward and combines dense rewards with turn-level precise credit assignment, effectively alleviating the language-action gap in Driving VLAs. • DriveMA achieves a new state of the art on the WOD-E2E, competitive results on NAVSIM, and consistently outperforms representative reasoning-centric Driving VLAs. 22 Related Work 

2.1 Language Interfaces for Driving VLAs 

Recent language-based driving systems introduce natural language into end-to-end autonomous driving for instruction following, scene understanding, decision grounding, and interpretabil-ity [1, 2]. Following this direction, several Driving VLA methods adopt explicit reasoning as the intermediate interface between visual inputs and actions [13, 14, 15, 5, 10]. In particular, DriveVLM uses chain-of-thought reasoning to connect scene description, scene analysis, and hierarchical plan-ning [4], while AutoVLA unifies reasoning and action generation in an autoregressive framework with adaptive thinking modes [5]. These methods demonstrate the value of language for driving, but still rely on relatively verbose reasoning interfaces, which increase annotation cost, generation diffi-culty, and inference latency. A parallel line of work questions whether explicit reasoning is necessary for strong end-to-end driving [16, 17]. NoRD shows that competitive Driving VLA performance can be obtained without dense reasoning annotations, reducing token overhead and training cost [16]. In contrast to both reasoning-centric VLAs and language-free alternatives, our work studies a middle-ground interface: a concise, low-entropy, and verifiable meta-action. Meta-actions preserve language-domain semantic decision grounding, while avoiding the data, generation, and latency burden of long-chain reasoning. 

2.2 Language-Action Alignment 

Language-action alignment is a central challenge for applying VLA models to autonomous driving. SimLingo aligns language and actions through multi-task training and Action Dreaming, grounding vision-language understanding in closed-loop driving behaviors [9]. LinkVLA improves language-action coupling by unifying language and action tokens in a shared token space and introducing an action-understanding objective to learn bidirectional trajectory–language mappings [18]. Drive-R1 mitigates reasoning-planning misalignment mainly through autonomous-driving domain alignment with large-scale Visual Question Answering (VQA) and reasoning supervision [8]. Rather than relying on implicit alignment from auxiliary tasks or domain data, DriveMA exploits the verifiability of meta-actions to explicitly measure and encourage consistency between the model’s stated high-level decision and its generated trajectory.

3.3 Action-Centric Supervised Training 

Before training the full planning task, DriveMA first performs action-centric supervised pretraining to adapt the general VLM to autonomous driving and warm up the meta-action prediction task. This stage is motivated by prior Driving VLA studies showing that domain-specific alignment is important for transferring general VLMs to driving scenarios [5, 8, 10]. 

Driving Action Alignment Pretraining. This stage contains two types of supervision. First, we use meta-action prediction data automatically generated from expert trajectories, where the model is trained to predict the expert meta-action from the driving input only, i.e., x → m∗, without generating future waypoints. This provides a direct warmup for the intermediate decision interface. Second, we incorporate 240K action-related Driving VQA samples from public datasets, selecting samples related to driving intention, action decision, and risk-aware behavior. These samples further align the general VLM with driving-domain perception and decision knowledge. 

Meta-Action-Conditioned Trajectory SFT. After action alignment pretraining, we train the full meta-action-guided planning task, where each sample follows the multi-turn target sequence x →

m∗ → τ ∗. This stage teaches the model to condition trajectory generation on the expert meta-action decision.

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experiments Setup Datasets. We evaluate DriveMA on two end-to-end driving benchmarks: WOD-E2E [11] and NAVSIM [12]. WOD-E2E focuses on challenging long-tail scenarios such as construction zones and safety-critical interactions, and we primarily report its official preference-based metric, RFS. NAVSIM is a planning-oriented autonomous driving benchmark built on OpenScene/nuPlan, and we primarily report PDMS, a closed-loop metric covering safety, progress, and comfort. In addition, we use open-source driving VQA datasets, including WaymoQA [21], IDKB [22], and LingoQA [23], comprising a total of 240K samples, for action-centric pretraining. 5Implementation Details. For Waymo, we downsample the provided 10Hz data by a factor of five for training, and DriveMA predicts 5s future trajectories at 1Hz. During RL, we use the official preference subset with 479 expert-scored trajectory samples and instantiate Rtraj with RFS. For NAVSIM, DriveMA predicts 4s future trajectories at 1Hz, uses the official trainval split with 103K samples, and holds out 18K samples for RL with Rtraj instantiated by PDMS. For both benchmarks, the model takes only current-frame images from the three front-facing cameras as visual input. DriveMA is built on the Qwen3.5 model family [24]; training hyperparameters and prompt formats are provided in the appendix.

4.3 Meta-Action Granularity Analysis 

We analyze the temporal granularity of meta-actions on the WOD-E2E validation subset. We vary the longitudinal chunk size from a single 5s action to finer multi-step actions, while keeping the lateral component as one 5s action, since lateral prediction is nearly saturated whereas longitudi-nal decisions remain the main bottleneck. Each setting is evaluated with 5s FDE across 2B, 4B, and 9B models under both predicted and oracle meta-action settings, where meta-actions are either generated by the model or replaced with ground-truth labels. As shown in Fig. 4, finer-grained chunks consistently improve oracle performance, but this gain does not transfer to the predicted setting. Instead, predicted performance across model scales converges around the 5s one-step meta-action, where the 4B model already reaches the saturation point and scaling to 9B brings negligible additional benefit. This is because smaller chunks rapidly enlarge the decision space: Table 3 shows that observed longitudinal action patterns increase from 4 to 221 as the chunk size decreases from 5s to 1s. Thus, fine-grained meta-actions provide stronger oracle guidance but are harder to predict reliably. We therefore adopt the 4B one-step setting as the primary DriveMA configuration, while also reporting the 2B variant since it already achieves state-of-the-art performance on WOD-E2E. 

4.4 Comparison of language interfaces 

We compare one-step meta-action with open-ended and structured reasoning on the WOD-E2E vali-dation subset, using Qwen3.5-2B, 4B, and 9B under both predicted and oracle settings. The reason-ing annotations are built with a drafting-reflection-correction pipeline, and their final decision step is derived from the same meta-action labels. To avoid unfairly disadvantaging reasoning interfaces due to insufficient optimization [34], we train all language-interface variants with 8 epochs of SFT and report the best checkpoint selected on the validation subset. Table 4 shows that one-step meta-action consistently performs best under predicted inference across all model scales. In addition to better prediction accuracy, meta-action also reduces inference la-tency. Structured reasoning is stronger than open-ended reasoning, but both lag behind meta-action when generated by the model itself. Under oracle inference, reasoning surpasses meta-action only at larger scales, indicating that reasoning offers higher potential but suffers from both generation and utilization bottlenecks. 4.5 Ablation Study of Training Strategy 

Table 5 analyzes the contribution of each training component. Action-centric pretraining provides a strong foundation for meta-action-guided planning, improving RFS Overall from 7.804 to 7.893 and RFS Spotlight from 6.832 to 7.189 over direct SFT. The larger gain on Spotlight suggests better generalization to challenging long-tail scenarios. Turn-level credit assignment further improves over vanilla GRPO under comparable reward supervision, increasing RFS Overall from 7.978 to 8.060 by precisely assigning rewards to their corresponding generation turns. The reward ablation shows that dense language-action supervision is crucial. Adding Rcons sub-stantially improves L-A Consistency from 88.50% to 98.80%, indicating that the consistency re-ward effectively aligns predicted trajectories with stated meta-actions. As qualitatively shown in Fig. 5, DriveMA better translates correct high-level meta-actions into executable trajectories in both longitudinal and lateral behaviors. The full reward combination achieves the best overall planning performance, suggesting that trajectory quality, action correctness, and language-action consistency are complementary. 

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we rethink language interfaces for Driving VLAs and show that concise one-step meta-actions provide an effective alternative to long-chain natural-language reasoning. By combining verifiable meta-action supervision with turn-level credit-assignment RL, DriveMA improves meta-action prediction, trajectory quality, and trajectory–meta-action consistency, achieving a new state of the art on WOD-E2E and competitive performance on NAVSIM. We hope this work encourages future Driving VLA research to focus not only on making language interfaces more expressive, but also on making them more verifiable, efficient, and actionable for end-to-end planning. 8References
