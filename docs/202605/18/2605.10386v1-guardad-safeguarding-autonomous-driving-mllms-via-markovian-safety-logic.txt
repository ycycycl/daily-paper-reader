## GUARD AD: Safeguarding Autonomous Driving MLLMs via Markovian Safety Logic

Abstract 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) are increasingly integrated into autonomous driving (AD) systems; however, they remain vulnerable to diverse safety threats, particu-larly in accident-prone scenarios. Recent safe-guard mechanisms have shown promise by in-corporating logical constraints, yet most rely on static formulations that lack temporally grounded safety reasoning over evolving traf-fic interactions, resulting in limited robustness in dynamic driving environments. To address these limitations, we propose G UARD AD, a model-agnostic safeguard that formulates AD safety as an evolving Markovian logical state. GUARD AD introduces Neuro-Symbolic Logic Formalization, which represents safety pred-icates over heterogeneous traffic participants and continuously induces them via n-th order Markovian Logic Induction. This design en-ables the inference of emerging and latent haz-ards beyond single-step observations. Rather than simply vetoing unsafe actions, G UARD AD performs Logic-Driven Action Revision, where inferred safety states actively guide action re-finement without modifying the underlying MLLM. Extensive experiments on multiple benchmarks and AD-MLLMs demonstrate that GUARD AD substantially reduces accident rates (-32.07%) while slightly improving task perfor-mance (+6.85%). Moreover, closed-loop simu-lation evaluations, together with physical-world vehicle studies, further validate the effective-ness and potential of G UARD AD.

3 Related Work 

AD-relevant models face significant security issues (Zhang et al., 2024b,d; Liu et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2025c; Liu et al., 2026, 2025d; Kong et al., 2025). Safety enhancements for AD-relevant mod-els include adversarial training (Zhang et al., 2023, 2024a; Lu et al., 2025), fine-tuning under perturba-tions (Liao et al., 2025; Oh et al., 2024), and sim-ulation testing (Cai et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025c). While effective, they mainly target perception-level errors (Liang et al., 2024) rather than grounded safety reasoning over evolving traffic interactions. Logic-driven safeguards have been studied in em-bodied agents (Sheng et al., 2024; Zhen et al., 2024; Haoyu et al., 2025; Xiang et al., 2025; Qin et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024, 2025b; Liang et al., 2025), but they are largely designed for specific tasks. For AD-MLLMs, SafeAuto (Zhang et al., 2025a) is the closest prior work, using visual structured knowledge and fixed traffic rules to correct high-level decisions. However, SafeAuto is ego- and rule-centric, largely static, and does not model tem-porally evolving risks; moreover, it is tightly cou-pled to a specific backbone and interface, limiting transferability across AD-MLLMs. As a result, general safeguards for AD-MLLMs in dynamic, multi-agent driving remain underexplored. 2Comparisons. GUARD AD differs from prior works by targeting the dynamic, interactive nature of AD instead of enforcing static traffic rules, and by incorporating temporal context beyond single-step observations. It is also model-agnostic rather than a specialized, model-coupled design. 

4 Method 

Motivation. Recent methods (Zhang et al., 2025a) mitigate risky actions, but they are often based on static rules and fail to capture the dynam-ics of driving environments. Motivated by these, we proposed G UARD AD (as shown in Fig. 2), con-ceptualize AD safety not as a set of static, instanta-neous constraints, but as a logical state that evolves with the interaction history between the ego vehicle and environment. G UARD AD is a model-agnostic safeguard that formulates AD safety as an evolv-ing Markovian logical state. It encodes Markovian Safety Logic and updates the state with an n-th order Markovian Logic Induction, then performs Logic-driven Action Revision to refine unsafe ac-tions without modifying the AD-MLLMs.

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup Models and baselines. We evaluate three widely used AD-MLLMs: DriveLM (Sima et al., 2024), Dolphins (Ma et al., 2024), and EM-VLM4AD (Gopalkrishnan et al., 2024). For safeguards, we compare G UARD AD (GA) against the closest base-line, SafeAuto (SA) (Zhang et al., 2025a). We also include two representative safeguards from embod-ied agents, RoboFactory (RF) (Qin et al., 2025) and Code-as-Monitor (CM) (Zhou et al., 2025). 

Benchmarks. We conduct experiments on two benchmarks. For safety-critical scenarios, we use VRU-Accident (Kim et al., 2025), which collects accident cases targeting AD-MLLMs. For common driving queries and decision-making, we use Driv-eLM (Sima et al., 2024), a widely used benchmark for AD-MLLMs evaluation. 

Evaluation Metrics. We report metrics in two dimensions. For task performance, we use GPT Score (GS ↑) and Qwen Score (QS ↑), where re-sponses are graded by GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) and Qwen-3 (Yang et al., 2025), respectively. For safety evaluation on VRU-Accident, we define a benchmark-specific metric, accident rate, to quan-tify the fraction of cases that result in an accident given the AD-MLLMs outputs; it is measured via 

GPT Accident Rate (GAR ↓) and Qwen Accident Rate (QAR ↓). Each experiment is repeated 10 times, and the average results are reported. Details of the Accident Rate are in the Appendix. 

Implementation Details. At each step, the model input includes an observation history of length k=2 . For temporal safety reasoning, we aggregate logical states with an n-th order window (n=4 ). When a violation is detected, we use GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) to generate an auxiliary prompt using a fixed verbalization template and re-queries the same AD-MLLMs under identical decoding; otherwise, the original action is kept. All experiments are run on a server with a 128-core Intel Xeon 8358 CPU (2.60 GHz), 1 TB RAM, and 8×NVIDIA A800 80 GB PCIe GPUs.

We report the main results of G UARD AD on both safety-critical and common benchmarks. 

5.2.1 Results in Accident-prone Scenarios

❶ Without safeguards, all AD-MLLMs show accident rates above 50%, with an average GAR of 68.76% and QAR of 76.48%, indicating sub-stantial safety risks and a clear need for effective enhancement methods for AD-MLLMs. 

❷ Across all safeguards, G UARD AD yields the largest degradation in accident rate, with average drops of 32.07% (GAR) and 35.54% (QAR), com-pared to only 5.34% and 6.84% on average for others. These re-sults indicate that G UARD AD improves task perfor-mance while substantially reducing accident risk.

❹ We further analyze failure cases. Results show that a large fraction of SafeAuto’s remaining acci-dents (20.99%) are triggered by the sudden appear-ance of traffic participants (e.g., pedestrians). 8 Conclusion and Future Work 

We presented G UARD AD, a model-agnostic safe-guard for AD-MLLMs that formulates AD safety 8as an evolving Markovian logical state. It encodes Neuro-Symbolic Logic Formalization and updates the state with an n-th order Markovian Logic In-duction, then performs Logic-Driven Action Re-vision to refine unsafe actions without modifying the AD-MLLMs. Extensive experiments indicate GUARD AD’s effectiveness and applicability. 

Future Work. ❶ Improve grounding and pars-ing ( e.g., entity tracking, predicate extraction). ❷

Expand predicates to cover ego decision error. 

Limitations 

While the G UARD AD shows promising results, sev-eral limitations remain. ❶ Its effectiveness relies on accurate entity, predicate, and action grounding; perception or extraction errors may propagate and cause unresolved failures. ❷ The current predicate and rule set does not fully capture all ego-centric de-cision errors or open-ended driving intents. ❸ The revision process incurs additional computational overhead and depends on external generators. 

Ethics Statement 

This paper proposes G UARD AD, a safeguard that reduces unsafe decisions made by autonomous-driving multimodal large language models through Markovian Safety Logic, rather than enhancing ca-pabilities that could be misused. The study relies on widely used autonomous driving benchmarks and closed-loop simulation, and does not involve the collection of sensitive personal data or any form of profiling. A small human-in-the-loop evalua-tion is conducted in a controlled laboratory setting with an experienced driver operating the vehicle; model behavior is assessed offline using logged observations, keeping participant risk minimal and avoiding exposure from real-world deployment.
