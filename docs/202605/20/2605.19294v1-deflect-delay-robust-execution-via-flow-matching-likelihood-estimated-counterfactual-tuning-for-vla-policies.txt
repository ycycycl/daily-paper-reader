Abstract: Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies are typically deployed with asynchronous inference: the robot executes a previously predicted action chunk while the model computes the next one. This creates a prediction-execution mis-alignment : the chunk is conditioned on the observation taken before inference began, but executes in a physical state that has already drifted forward by sev-eral control steps; naive asynchronous rollover collapses from 89% to under 1% on Kinetix as the inference cycle covers up to seven control steps. We introduce DEFLECT 1, a fully offline post-training refinement that applies as a near drop-in upgrade to existing async-VLA stacks by converting latency itself into a label-free preference signal: counterfactual fresh/stale action pairs are constructed from a frozen reference policy and scored under the deployment-time conditioning via an implicit flow-matching likelihood-ratio surrogate, with no human labels, reward models, or online rollouts. DEFLECT substantially extends the usable delay en-velope of async VLA control, with +6 .4 success-rate gain in the high-latency regime (5–7 control steps), +4 .6 when transferred to a real-scale VLA at the longest delay, and consistent improvements on two real-robot tasks (a bimanual conveyor pick-and-place and a reactive whack-a-mole). This prediction-execution misalignment is intrinsic to async control under non-trivial inference latency. Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies that map observations and language directly to continu-ous action chunks have become a default recipe for general-purpose embodied control [1, 2, 3, 4]. However, their inference latency is too long to support tight closed-loop execution at typical control rates. To mitigate this, modern deployments employ asynchronous inference : the robot continues executing a previously predicted action chunk while the model computes the next one. While this overlaps inference with execution, scene changes during inference can readily turn inference latency into a prediction-execution mismatch. By the time the newly computed chunk begins to execute, the scene may already have drifted away from the observation that conditioned it, leaving the policy to act on a systematically stale snapshot. As a result, even minor mismatches between the conditioning observation and the execution-time state can accumulate into large trajectory errors during chunk execution. Figure 2 illustrates this mismatch on a real-robot pharmaceutical-filling task. The policy predicts a pellet release when the vial reaches the target pour position, but by execution time the vial has already moved several centimeters down the belt, causing the pellets to miss the target and the trial to fail. The same delay-induced failure appears systematically in simulation: on Kinetix, naive async, which simply executes each newly computed chunk as it is ready, collapses from 89% 

to under 1% as inference delay grows to seven control steps, and state-of-the-art runtime mitiga-tions only partially recover. This sharp delay scaling suggests that latency is not merely a runtime robustness problem. Under stale observations, multiple candidate chunks may be plausible, but only some remain consistent with the execution-time scene; supervised fine-tuning (SFT) minimizes per-example reconstruction and has no mechanism to rank these temporal counterfactuals. Latency is therefore a preference-learning problem that the SFT objective is structurally unequipped to solve. Existing delay-robust VLA methods leave this training-time preference gap unresolved due to three main limitations. First, methods like A2C2 [5], RTC [6], and BID [7] rely exclusively on post-2hoc runtime adjustments (e.g., residual heads, inpainting, or resampling); because these approaches operate on a frozen base policy, they fail to train the model to recognize which candidate chunks re-main dynamically valid, causing them to collapse on highly dynamic tasks in high-latency regimes. Second, methods incorporating training-time interventions remain restricted to behavior cloning: VLASH [8] modifies training conditioning via state roll-forward, and AsyncVLA [9] trains a con-fidence rater based on expert-MSE for selective regeneration, but these techniques merely force the model to blindly match expert actions under uncertainty and fundamentally lack a preference-based mechanism to penalize actions that become detrimental under stale conditions. Third, existing preference-alignment objectives are not delay-aware: alignment signals based on fresh condition-ing [10, 11, 12, 13] are systematically misaligned with the test-time distribution, since an action labeled “preferred” under a fresh observation may be strictly suboptimal under stale conditioning, leading matched-compute baselines like PFM [12] to exhibit negative transfer at large delays. Ul-timately, because flow-matching policies lack an analytic action density, standard direct preference optimization (DPO) [14] log-likelihood-ratio objectives cannot be imported directly. A delay-aware refinement must simultaneously provide latency-induced preference pairs, a deployment-context scoring rule, and a flow-compatible likelihood surrogate. 

We present DEFLECT, a fully offline post-training refinement that converts latency itself into a label-free preference signal. Our key insight is that stale observations in async inference create fresh-versus-stale action counterfactuals that can be used directly as preference pairs : the fresh-aligned rollout approximates the action the policy would choose from the execution-time scene, while the stale-aligned rollout captures the delayed-execution alternative. Concretely, DEFLECT queries a frozen reference VLA twice with the same sampling noise, once under a fresh-aligned context and once under a stale-aligned context. This yields a preferred action and a rejected action without human labels, reward models, or online rollouts. Both candidates are then scored under the single deployment-time context cmix , the conditioning the policy actually receives at test time, using an implicit flow-matching likelihood surrogate. The resulting DPO objective trains the policy to prefer the fresh-conditioned candidate, with an SFT anchor on expert chunks to prevent collapse. Inference is identical to the base policy: DEFLECT is a near drop-in refinement with no additional test-time cost. Our contributions are threefold: 1. Latency as counterfactual supervision. We turn latency from a deployment failure mode into an offline training signal. Replaying a trajectory under fresh and stale observations lets a frozen reference VLA identify the action chunk aligned with the execution-time scene versus the one induced by stale perception. 2. DEFLECT: label-free DPO for flow-matching VLAs. DEFLECT converts these counter-factual preferences into a flow-matching DPO update by scoring both candidate chunks under the deployment-time context cmix with an implicit likelihood surrogate. The refinement leaves inference unchanged and requires no human labels, reward model, or online rollouts. 3. Delay-robust validation across simulation and real-world deployment. DEFLECT improves over VLASH in the unseen high-delay regime on Kinetix ( +6 .4 for d∈{ 5, 6, 7}; +8 .0 over PFM), transfers to LIBERO ( +4 .6 at d=7 ), and is validated on bimanual conveyor pick-and-place and reactive whack-a-mole tasks. A 12 -task analysis further shows that the learned correc-tion is dynamics-aware, variance-preserving ( σ-ratio 0.97 ), and transferable from training delays 

d∈{ 1, 2} to d=7 .

Under asynchronous inference with a delay of d control steps, the chunk predicted from ct does not begin execution until t + d. The policy therefore acts in a physical state that is d steps newer than the observation that generated the chunk. When each inference cycle executes K actions before the next chunk is available, the first K entries of the chunk determine most of the executed behavior; this is the regime we target. Our base policy is a flow-matching policy [15, 16, 3, 4]: conditioned on (ct, l ),it learns a velocity field vθ (xτ , c t, l, τ ) whose ODE integration over τ ∈ [0 , 1] transports a Gaussian noise sample x0 into an action chunk x1. This structure supports chunk-level action generation and, importantly for DEFLECT, exposes an internal denoising trajectory whose velocity field can be reshaped offline by preference alignment. Pure SFT training of vθ has no native mechanism to rank competing future-consistent behaviors un-der stale observations: minimizing the per-example reconstruction loss does not encode preference between candidates that all fit the data. DEFLECT converts this gap into a pairwise supervision sig-nal by constructing counterfactual preference pairs from the same offline data, and by training the flow-matching policy to prefer the fresh-conditioned candidate over the stale-conditioned one un-der the observation it actually receives at deployment. Figure 3 illustrates the DEFLECT pipeline; Sec. 2.1 details how the preference pairs are constructed and Sec. 2.2 details how they are scored and optimized.

LIBERO [19], a real-scale manipulation benchmark with four suites that tests transfer to a π0.5-scale VLA; and three real-robot tasks on a bimanual arm setup. Unless stated otherwise, Kinetix numbers are averaged over 12 tasks × 1024 rollouts per cell and LIBERO over 500 episodes per suite per delay; all baselines use the released VLASH async5 checkpoint. Full per-benchmark setup, per-suite tables, per-λ sweeps, uncertainty estimates, and additional ablations are deferred to Appendix E–H.

## 4 Related Work 

VLA policies and delay-robust execution. Modern VLA policies span several architectural fami-lies: autoregressive discrete-token decoders (RT-2 [1], OpenVLA [2]); cross-embodiment generalist transformers (Octo [22], GR00T N1 [23]); continuous-action heads based on CVAE (ACT [24]) or diffusion (Diffusion Policy [25], RDT-1B [26]); and flow-matching policies ( π0 [3], π0.5 [4]). The last family is the class we target. Inference latency in such policies has been tackled along two complementary axes. Runtime-side methods operate on a frozen base policy at deployment: RTC in-paints the residual chunk prefix at inference boundaries [6], BID performs closed-loop action resam-pling under forward-contrast and backward-coherence criteria [7], A2C2 applies a correction head at test time [5], streaming planners emit actions before a full chunk is ready [27], and systems-level analyses identify the bottlenecks [28]. Training-time interventions instead modify the supervised pipeline itself: VLASH rolls the proprioceptive state forward at training to align prediction with the execution-time state [8], and AsyncVLA combines an asynchronous flow-matching objective with a learned confidence rater that triggers partial chunk regeneration [9]. Both lines, however, remain behavior-cloning objectives and lack a preference mechanism over stale-conditioned actions. Asyn-chronous control under inference latency has also been studied in deep RL [29, 30]. DEFLECT is orthogonal to all of these: it is a post-training refinement of the policy parameters through prefer-ence optimization, leaves the inference procedure unchanged, and is therefore composable with any runtime scheduler. 

Preference optimization for continuous policies. Preference optimization originated in deep RL from human preferences [31] and matured into RLHF [32] and DPO [14, 33], with subsequent variants such as IPO [34], SimPO [35], and KTO [36]. The paradigm has been extended to dif-fusion [13], VLA refinement with human labels [10], robust diffusion-policy fine-tuning under corrupted feedback [37, 38], online flow-policy optimization [11], and Preference Flow Matching (PFM) [12], which trains an add-on flow module from expert-vs-policy preferences on clean obser-vations. We differ in two ways: (i) we target fully offline flow-matching VLA post-training rather than online RL, and (ii) our preference labels are generated automatically from latency-induced fresh/stale counterfactuals rather than from human labels, reward models, or clean-observation ex-pert comparisons. As we show in Table 1, the latter distinction is not cosmetic: under stale observa-tions a generic clean-obs preference signal (PFM) causes negative transfer relative to the base policy at d ≥ 2.85 Conclusion 

We presented DEFLECT, a fully offline refinement that converts inference delay into counterfac-tual preference supervision for flow-matching VLAs. The learned correction is dynamics-aware and variance-preserving rather than mode-collapsing, extending the usable delay envelope of asyn-chronous π0.5-style VLAs into the regime where runtime-scheduling baselines collapse, all without changing inference, adding a scheduler, or requiring human or reward labels. 

## 6 Limitations and Future Work 

Limitations. Fresh/stale counterfactual labels are noisy, so DPO-specific gains concentrate in the high-delay regime and low-delay improvements remain modest, partially attributable to training-restart effects. Our evidence is heaviest in simulation plus a small real-robot pilot; transfer to diffu-sion or autoregressive action heads and to jitter-dominated latency regimes remains open. 

Future work. The counterfactual-supervision pattern underlying DEFLECT, contrasting an ideal-but-undeployable condition with the deployable one and scoring both under the deployment context, is not specific to latency. The same recipe should apply wherever an ideal/deployable gap can be simulated offline, e.g., full-precision vs. quantized action heads, full-sensor vs. sensor-dropout perception, or teacher vs. distilled policies, with the “ideal” branch acting as a zero-cost preference oracle for the deployable branch.

## D Cross-Conditional Generation vs. Scoring (Theoretical Remark) 

The preference pair (A+, A −) is generated under two distinct observations (cfresh  

> t

, c stale  

> t

) via Eq. (2), but the implicit-likelihood scores rθ (A±) and rref (A±) in Eq. (4) are all evaluated under a third 

observation cmix  

> t

. This cross-conditional structure (absent from the original LLM-DPO derivation, in which a single prompt conditions both generation and scoring) is a deliberate design choice, not a modeling inconsistency, and we justify it here from three complementary angles. 

(i) Deployment-matched scoring. At inference time the deployed asynchronous policy sees ex-actly cmix  

> t

: robot proprioception is a roll-forward estimate of the execution-time state produced by VLASH (cf. Sec. 2.1), and environment channels are stale (rendered when the inference window started). The behavior that the training loss must shape is therefore πθ (· | cmix  

> t

), not πθ (· | cfresh  

> t

)

nor πθ (· | cstale  

> t

), since neither counterfactual observation is ever available at test time. If we had scored under cfresh , we would be optimizing a hypothetical ideal-observer policy that does not exist in deployment; scoring under cstale instead would optimize the naive-asynchronous baseline whose failure modes we are explicitly trying to move away from. Scoring under cmix is the only choice consistent with the deployment distribution of the output policy. 

(ii) Temporal counterfactual as a paired-preference labeler. The fresh-observer rollout A+ and the stale-observer rollout A− act jointly as a fully-offline labeling source: on each generated pair, the temporal counterfactual tells the training loop which of the two candidates is the fresh-observer choice, and the DPO loss pushes πθ (· | cmix ) to assign higher likelihood to A+ than to A−. We make no claim, theoretical or empirical, that this drives πθ (· | cmix ) to globally match a hypothetical fresh-conditioned policy π⋆(· | cfresh ) — the two condition on different contexts and are not inter-changeable. The role of π⋆ here is only as a paired-preference labeler, not as a target distribution. 

(iii) Bradley–Terry structure is preserved. The Bradley–Terry model underlying DPO requires only that (a) the pair (A+, A −) is fixed before scoring, and (b) both scores condition on a single shared context [14]. Our construction satisfies both. The generating contexts (cfresh , c stale ) deter-mine the label assignment (which action plays the role of the chosen A+ and which the rejected 

A−) but do not enter the score function; the reference-calibrated margin in Eq. (4) evaluates rθ and 

rref symmetrically under the single context cmix  

> t

. The standard DPO gradient-analysis guarantees therefore carry over, with the only substitution being that the conditioning variable throughout the Bradley–Terry likelihood is cmix  

> t

rather than a raw user prompt. Taken together, the three angles give the same operational statement: we use the fresh-observation oracle only as an offline labeler for paired preferences, and the trained policy reads only the real 

cmix  

> t

at deployment. The loss shapes πθ (· | cmix ) to prefer the fresh-conditioned candidate over the stale-conditioned candidate on the generated pairs .

(iv) Direct empirical test. The three arguments above are complemented by a direct controlled ablation on Kinetix. We train a matched-context variant that is identical to DEFLECT in every respect (same pairs, same noise, same λDPO =0 .02 , same 24-epoch cosine schedule) except that it scores each action under its own generating context: rθ (A+) is evaluated at cfresh and rθ (A−) at 

cstale . Our hypothesis is that scoring both branches under the deployment-time cmix , rather than under each branch’s own generating context, is what aligns the policy with the conditioning it actu-ally sees at test time; under this hypothesis, the matched variant should systematically underperform DEFLECT. 14 60

Preference Flow Matching [12] was originally proposed for general flow-based generative-policy alignment and is not a VLA method. For a fair comparison we re-implement it within our VLA training pipeline. Conceptually, PFM trains an add-on flow module on top of a frozen reference policy using (expert, reference) preference pairs drawn on clean observations, then corrects the refer-ence’s chunk at test time by integrating its own flow conditioned on the test-time (stale) observation. Our re-implementation is matched to DEFLECT in every respect (same F LOW POLICY architec-ture, same VLASH async5 initialization, same Kinetix dataset, same 24-epoch cosine schedule, same compute), so the only controlled differences are (i) the preference-pair construction (expert-vs-policy on clean o for PFM vs. fresh-vs-stale counterfactual for DEFLECT) and (ii) the loss (Optimal-Transport Conditional Flow Matching, OT-CFM, for PFM vs. the DPO loss in Eq. (4) for DEFLECT). OT-CFM is the flow-matching regression loss used by PFM. Compared to standard CFM (where each noise sample and each target action are paired independently per minibatch), OT-CFM pairs them via a per-batch optimal-transport plan, which yields straighter ODE trajectories under finite-batch training. The hyperparameter σ controls a Gaussian perturbation around this OT path: σ=0 uses the deterministic OT coupling (each noise sample is matched to a single target); 

σ=0 .1 (the PFM-paper default) adds mild Gaussian noise around the OT path, which regularizes the finite-batch OT plan against sample noise (with limited batch sizes the OT plan itself is noisy, and σ> 0 smooths over this). We report both variants to rule out the possibility that PFM’s under-performance vs DEFLECT is a σ-mistuning artifact. Table 8: PFM vs. DEFLECT on Kinetix (success rate %). ## K Mechanism: What DEFLECT Changes in the Flow-Matching Policy 

To isolate what DEFLECT changes in the policy, we compare it against VLASH under matched inputs: for each of the 12 Kinetix tasks at d=6 , we pick 4 rollout states where the two policies disagree on the action and integrate both denoising ODEs from the same Gaussian noise seed ( 48 

paired trajectories per panel). The same-noise protocol isolates the policy difference from envi-ronment stochasticity. Selecting disagreement states is deliberate, and upper-bounds the deflection magnitudes we report; on typical states, corrections will be smaller. The two qualitative signatures of the correction — which task types receive larger corrections, and whether the action distribution collapses — are properties of the policies themselves and survive the selection rule. Variance preser-vation is in fact strengthened by the selection: even at maximally-divergent states, DEFLECT does not collapse the action distribution. ## L Restart-Effect Decomposition (VLASH vs VLASH-RETRAINED vs DEFLECT) 

Any method that resumes training from a pretrained checkpoint inherits a non-trivial lift from new optimizer state and LR warmup; we call this the restart/cosine effect . To give reviewers a transpar-ent decomposition of DEFLECT’s delay-averaged +3 .9 lift over VLASH, we train an additional dedicated control VLASH-RETRAINED : the same VLASH checkpoint, the same 24 -epoch cosine schedule, the same optimizer and batch size as DEFLECT, but with λDPO =0 (i.e., pure SFT con-tinuation with no preference term).

## N Training-Time Cost of DEFLECT 

DEFLECT does not change inference at all: the flow-matching ODE is solved exactly as for the VLASH base policy. It does add an offline training cost relative to simply continuing SFT. The per-step cost of the DEFLECT update is dominated by two additional forward passes through the reference policy to sample A+ and A− (line 4 of Algorithm 1); both are wrapped in stop gradient ,so they do not propagate gradients and do not increase the gradient-step compute. The velocity-field forward/backward pass itself is structurally identical to vanilla SFT: the DPO term and the SFT anchor reuse the same vθ evaluation on x+ 

> τ

and x− 

> τ

that the flow-matching loss already computes on the expert chunk. Because this overhead is entirely offline and inference is unchanged, DEFLECT remains a near-drop-in replacement for deployed VLASH checkpoints. 24
