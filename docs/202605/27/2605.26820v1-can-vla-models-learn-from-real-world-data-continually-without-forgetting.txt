## Abstract 

Vision-language-action (VLA) models provide a promising foundation for general-purpose robotics. However, their successful deployment in real-world scenarios requires the ability to continually acquire new skills while retaining previously learned behaviors. While pioneering research has studied the continual learning of VLA models in narrowly simulated environments, this challenge remains largely unexplored under realistic con-ditions. To address this limitation, we construct a real-world continual learning dataset comprising four sequential manipulation tasks, spanning rigid-object pick-and-place, contact-rich pressing, and deformable-object folding. Using this dataset, we conduct comprehensive experiments and find that VLA models suffer significant catastrophic forgetting when continually learning from heterogeneous real-world demonstrations. We then systematically evaluate experience replay and uncover key implementation factors that govern its success. In summary, this work provides the first empirical study of real-world continual VLA learning and offers practical guidance for deploying long-lived robot policies.

## 1 Introduction 

Vision-language-action (VLA) models have emerged as a promising paradigm for general-purpose robotics by integrating visual perception, language understanding, and action generation into a unified policy [ 1, 2]. Trained on large-scale robot data, they exhibit strong transfer and cross-task generalization for embodied control [ 3]. However, real-world deployment requires more than strong one-time performance, where robots must continually acquire new skills from evolving data without losing previously learned capabilities. This challenge is closely tied to catastrophic forgetting, a long-standing issue in continual learning where adaptation to new tasks can overwrite prior knowledge [ 4, 5]. For VLA models, sequential updates on new robot data risk overwriting previously learned skills, yet continuously retraining on the entire accumulated dataset is often prohibitively expensive. Consequently, mitigating catastrophic forgetting becomes indispensable for scalable, lifelong robot learning. Recent studies have begun to investigate continual learning in VLA models from multiple perspectives. For example, [ 6] shows that large pretrained VLAs can be surprisingly resistant to forgetting, with pretraining, model scale, and even small replay buffers substantially improving retention during sequential learning. Furthermore, [ 7] suggests that sequential fine-tuning with on-policy RL can maintain a favorable balance between retention, plasticity, and generalization in large VLA models.

The bottom panels summa-rize our central findings: (1) naive sequential fine-tuning causes severe catastrophic forgetting; (2) a modest replay budget largely mitigates this forgetting; and (3) implementation-level factors—replay frequency, buffer size, and action normalization—can determine whether continual learning succeeds or collapses entirely. In parallel, emerging adaptation methods [ 8, 9] aim to mitigate forgetting under sequential task arrival and limited access to historical data, while standard supervised fine-tuning remains prone to degrading previously acquired capabilities in long-horizon robotic learning settings. Despite these advances, existing continual learning studies on VLA models are constrained by several limitations that overestimate their robustness to forgetting. First, simulation-based benchmarks exhibit substantially less heterogeneity than real-world deployment. Widely used benchmarks such as LIBERO [ 10 ] share fixed embodiment, simulator, and action space across narrowly defined sub-tasks. Such limited distribution shifts are likely to underestimate forgetting, as retention on correlated tasks may not generalize to heterogeneous real-world deployment. Additionally, replay-based robustness may be confounded by pretraining overlap. As [ 6 ] suggests, forgetting resistance attributed to experience replay may instead stem from large-scale pretraining. Since LIBERO is widely involved in many VLA pretraining datasets, it is unclear whether strong performance reflects effective retention or merely reactivated memorized priors. Furthermore, existing protocols violate causality. Several studies pre-compute global statistics ( e.g. , normalization parameters) over the entire task stream before sequential training, leaking future information into the learning process. Reported success may thus be an artifact of an informationally privileged regime unavailable in real deployment. These issues raise questions about whether current findings generalize to realistic settings and reveal an unexamined question: whether forgetting in VLA models stems primarily from algorithmic design failures or from overlooked factors such as distributional inconsistency, normalization drift, and optimization imbalance under real-world sequential data. Inspired by the discussions above, we shift the evaluation of continual learning in VLA models from simulation to the physical world. We collect a real-world sequential manipulation dataset and conduct a systematic study under deployment-realistic constraints. Our contributions are threefold: • We develop a real-world continual learning dataset comprising four sequential manipulation tasks (500 trajectories for each task), spanning rigid-object pick-and-place to deformable-object folding, with substantial inter-task variation in object geometry, grasping strategy, and motion primitive. Equipped with this dataset, we show that VLA models exhibit significant catastrophic forgetting when continually learning from heterogeneous real-world demonstrations. 2• We further evaluate the effectiveness of the experience replay-based approach under real-world sequential data. Notably, we show that the implementation details often overlooked in prior work, such as replay frequency and action normalization strategy, can significantly affect the continual learning process. • Beyond these two findings, we find that well-configured sequential learning with modest replay can outperform joint multi-task training under equivalent data budgets. Joint training fails due to gradient interference, representation domination by visually richer tasks, and imbalanced loss scales. In contrast, sequential learning naturally avoids this by staging task acquisition with dedicated learning phases, while replay preserves prior knowledge without inducing destructive competition. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Vision-Language-Action Models 

In recent years, VLA models have attracted growing attention for their ability to couple visual observations, language understanding, and action generation within a unified policy. Representative systems such as OpenVLA [ 11 ], π0 [ 12 ], and π0.5 [ 13 ] demonstrate the potential of scaling VLA policies with large-scale robot data and foundation model priors. Beyond standard single policy formulations, RDT-1B [ 14 ] explores diffusion-based foundation policies for bimanual manipula-tion, X-VLA [ 15 ] studies scalable cross-embodiment modeling, and WorldVLA [ 16 ] introduces an action-centric world modeling perspective. More recently, LLaVA-VLA [ 17 ] rethinks the practical deployment of VLA models by providing a comprehensive benchmark and an improved baseline with stronger multi-view perception and action modeling. AtomVLA [ 18 ] further investigates scalable post-training by decomposing atomic subtasks and using predictive latent world models to improve long-horizon manipulation. Despite these advances, most existing VLA works focus on static pretraining, finetuning, or post-training settings. In contrast, our work studies whether VLA models can continually adapt to sequentially arriving real-world robot data without catastrophic forgetting. 

2.2 Continual Learning 

Continual learning aims to acquire knowledge from a stream of incoming data or tasks over time, while preserving previously learned capabilities and remaining adaptable to future changes [ 4, 5 , 19 ]. In contrast to typical learning scenarios that assume a fixed training set and a stationary test distribution, continual learning considers sequential, potentially non-stationary settings in which the learner must update itself as the distribution shifts. Research in this field typically highlights three fundamental characteristics: stability [ 20 ], which refers to the system’s ability to preserve existing knowledge without suffering from catastrophic forgetting; plasticity [ 21 ], which describes its capacity to absorb new information and master new tasks; and generalization [ 22 ], which represents its ability to apply learned knowledge both within individual tasks and across a sequence of incrementally evolving tasks. Extensive efforts have been devoted to enhancing continual learning along these three dimensions. For example, experience replay-based approaches primarily enhance stability by maintaining a small memory of past samples and interleaving them with incoming data, thereby mitigating catastrophic forgetting. Representative examples include iCaRL [ 23 ], GEM [ 24 ], A-GEM [ 25 ], MIR [ 26 ], and ER/DER++ [20, 27]. In this paper, we investigate continual learning in VLA models with an emphasis on stability, which fundamentally governs their practical utility under sequential and non-stationary real-world conditions. Moreover, we identify that implementation-level factors, including replay frequency and action normalization, can shape stability more profoundly than algorithmic design.

4.2 Experimental Setup 

Model Baseline. We use π0.5 [ 3] as the base policy and follow its standard SFT setup: AdamW optimizer with cosine decay (peak learning rate 5 × 10 −5, warmup 200 steps, decay to 5 × 10 −6), batch size 128, EMA decay 0.998, and action horizon 10. Each task is trained for 4,000 steps. We evaluate model checkpoints after each training stage by rolling out the policy for each task and computing the mean score per task using the task-specific rubrics. Each raw score is normalized to a 0–100 scale by dividing by the maximum possible score for that task. 

Action Normalization. VLA models rely on task-specific action normalization statistics (μk, σ k) to standardize robot actions before training. In a continual setting, however, global statistics across all tasks are unavailable at the onset of training, since future tasks have not yet been observed. Therefore, we compare two strategies for action normalization. Strategy-I fixes the statistics of the first task 

(μ1, σ 1) throughout training, ensuring a consistent action space. Strategy-II uses individual statistics 

(μk, σ k) for each task, causing the normalization reference to shift. We adopt Strategy-I as the default configuration for all our experiments.

## 5 Effectiveness of Experience Replay 

The preceding section established that naive sequential fine-tuning leads to catastrophic forgetting in real-world VLA adaptation. We now investigate whether experience replay (ER) [ 20 ], a standard continual learning technique, can mitigate this collapse under realistic data budgets. The core idea is to maintain a replay buffer B of bounded capacity and interleave historical demonstrations with incoming data during training on each new task. The experiments are conducted to investigate the following research questions: • RQ3: Can the ER approach mitigate forgetting in real-world continual VLA adaptation? Replay Buffer Management. The replay buffer B has a fixed total capacity of M episodes, where 

M = B · |D k| and B is the buffer ratio relative to a single-task dataset. Upon completing training on the k-th task, a subset of its demonstrations is stored in B. Replay Frequency. To balance new task learning and forgetting regularization, we control the proportion of replay samples via a hyperparameter fr ∈ (0 , 1) . At each training step, we construct a mini-batch by first sampling a source: with probability fr , we draw from the replay buffer B; with probability 1 − fr , we draw from the dataset of the current task. In practice, we set fr to a small value to prioritize learning the new task while incurring minimal computational overhead from replay. To maintain 4,000 effective optimization steps on the current task after replay is introduced, the total training budget is increased to 4000 /(1 − fr ) for replay-based methods. 

Model Training. By default, we adopt B = 0 .2 and fr = 0 .2, and the other training configuration is the same as the Section 4. Additionally, we run a hyperparameter sweep over buffer ratio B ∈{0.002 , 0.02 , 0.2} and replay frequency fr ∈ { 0.05 , 0.1, 0.2, 0.5}.

5.2 Results Analysis 

Experience replay eliminates catastrophic forgetting with modest budgets. As shown in Table 1, under the default configuration ( B = 0 .2, fr = 0 .2), all four tasks remain within 10 percentage points of their single-task baselines, and average NBT drops from 80.0 (No ER) to 5.0. Remarkably, a replay budget of just 20% of a single task’s data and 20% of training steps suffices to nearly eliminate forgetting. Beyond retention, the fold towel task exhibits a strong positive FT, rising from 52.0 (single-task) to 78–92 within the sequence, indicating effective rigid-to-deformable transfer. 

Figure 3: Forgetting matrices under sequential fine-tuning. Without ER (left), all previously learned tasks collapse to near-zero performance, confirming severe catastrophic forgetting. With appropriately configured ER (right panels), forgetting is largely eliminated across all tasks. However, excessively high replay frequency impairs new-task learning, while insufficient replay data weakens retention—revealing a U-shaped sensitivity that governs the stability–plasticity trade-off. 

Impact of buffer size and replay frequency. Larger buffers generally improve retention, though sensitivity varies by task. The hang cup task is most vulnerable, dropping from 97.5 to 60.0 as buffer size decreases, indicating that tasks requiring precise spatial localization demand diverse replay trajectories. Replay frequency exhibits a U-shaped trade-off: low frequency ( fr = 0 .05 ) fails to preserve old tasks, excessive frequency ( fr = 0 .5) suppresses new-task learning (press button drops to 33.3 at Task 3), while the best balance occurs near fr = 0 .2. In summary, the buffer size primarily controls the diversity of retained experience, whereas replay frequency modulates the stability-plasticity trade-off. 

Continual learning-based pipeline outperforms joint training. As shown in Table 1, the continual learning pipeline with replay consistently matches or exceeds joint training across all four tasks, especially for the press button task, where joint training collapses to 13.3. Joint training fails due to three interrelated mechanisms: (i) gradient interference, where updates for one task degrade another; (ii) representation domination, where tasks with richer visual features overshadow subtle ones; and (iii) task imbalance, where heterogeneous loss scales cause uneven capacity allocation. Continual learning avoids these failures by staging task acquisition with dedicated learning phases and a cleaner optimization landscape, while replay preserves prior tasks without inducing gradient competition. Action normalization significantly affects ER effectiveness . As demonstrated in Table 2, per-task normalization (Strategy-II) causes models to fail on non-stack bowl tasks, yet applying stack bowl’s statistics at test time partially restores performance, indicating that the model learns task semantics but fails to ground them in a stable spatial coordinate system. ## 6 Conclusion 

This paper presents the first real-world continual learning dataset for VLA adaptation and a systematic empirical study of replay-based methods under deployment-realistic constraints. Our results show that a modest replay budget nearly eliminates catastrophic forgetting when replay frequency and action normalization are properly configured. Critically, these low-level implementation details significantly govern the performance of continual adaptation, rather than minor engineering choices. Furthermore, we demonstrate that a well-configured sequential learning pipeline can outperform joint multi-task training, suggesting that continual adaptation offers a competitive paradigm for scalable real-world VLA learning. Still, there are remaining limitations of our work. We are currently conducting experiments on a single robot platform that performs four representative manipulation tasks. Extending the evaluation to include more embodiments and a broader range of task distributions would further strengthen the generality of our findings. Moreover, we primarily investigate replay-based continual learning and normalization consistency. We plan to introduce other continual adaptation strategies and larger-scale long-horizon scenarios in future work. This work is expected to inspire more subsequent research on the real-world lifelong learning of VLA models. 8References

Unlike binary success/failure metrics commonly used in simulation benchmarks, we adopt a fine-grained multi-stage evaluation protocol for all tasks. This design serves two purposes. First, it captures partial task completion, allowing us to distinguish between partial competence and complete failure, which is particularly important for analyzing the severity of forgetting during continual adaptation. Second, real-world evaluation is inherently noisy due to factors such as object pose variation, lighting changes, and execution stochasticity. Multi-stage scoring therefore provides more stable and informative measurements than binary success rates under limited evaluation trials. Each task is evaluated through a sequence of intermediate checkpoints, where each successfully completed checkpoint contributes one score unit. The maximum score differs across tasks depending on task complexity. 

A.1 Stack Bowl (D1) 

The robot must locate a yellow bowl, grasp it, and place it into a green bowl. The task is evaluated using four intermediate checkpoints (maximum score: 4): 1. The gripper approaches the yellow bowl within 3 cm. 2. The robot successfully grasps the yellow bowl. 3. The robot moves the bowl above the green bowl. 4. The robot successfully places the yellow bowl into the green bowl. A penalty of −0.5 is applied if the target bowl is knocked over during execution. 

A.2 Hang Cup (D2) 

The robot must grasp a purple cup and hang it onto a mug rack. The task is evaluated using four intermediate checkpoints (maximum score: 4): 1. The robot correctly locates and grasps the cup. 2. The robot moves the cup near the mug rack (within 3 cm). 3. The robot performs a valid hanging attempt with inward insertion motion. 4. The cup is successfully hung onto the rack. 

A.3 Press Button (D3) 

The robot must identify a green button, move the end-effector toward it, and press it successfully. The task is evaluated using three intermediate checkpoints (maximum score: 3): 1. The robot correctly localizes the button with proper end-effector orientation. 2. The end-effector moves near the button (within 3 cm). 3. The robot successfully presses the button. 

A.4 Fold Towel (D4) 

The robot must fold a gray towel corner-to-corner. The task is evaluated using five intermediate checkpoints (maximum score: 5): 1. The robot correctly locates the top-right towel corner. 2. The robot successfully grasps the corner. 3. The robot folds the corner toward the top-left corner. 4. The robot correctly locates the bottom-right corner. 5. The robot completes the final towel alignment and tidying behavior. 11
