# RECTOR: Priority-Aware Rule-Based Reranking for Compliance-Aware Autonomous Driving Trajectory Selection 

Hadi Hajieghrary 1, Benedikt Walter 2, Chaitanya Shinde 3, Paul Schmitt 4, and Miguel Hurtado 5

Abstract —Autonomous driving stacks must pick one trajectory from a multi-modal candidate set; choosing by model confidence ignores safety, traffic-law, and comfort constraints. We present RECTOR (Rule-Enforced Constrained Trajectory Orchestra-tor), a post-generation reranking layer that scores candidates against a tiered rulebook (Safety ≻ Legal ≻ Road ≻ Comfort) via differentiable proxies and a scene-conditioned applicability mechanism, then selects with a deterministic ε-lexicographic rule that preserves cross-tier priority by construction—without retraining the predictor. On the Waymo Open Motion Dataset 

validation_interactive split (43,219 augmented instances, K=6 ), under Protocol B (28-rule proxy catalog, oracle applicability) rule-aware selection cuts Safety+Legal violations from 28.58% to 20.42% and Total from 40.32% to 32.41% versus confidence-only on the same candidates. A uniform-weight weighted-sum baseline matches binary compliance on this benchmark—the empirical lift comes from rule-aware ranking, while the lexicographic guarantee is the structural differentiator no weight calibration can replicate. Under adversarial confidence corruption, confidence-only selection fails in 100% of scenarios while both rule-aware selectors reject the injected mode in ∼96%. All figures are proxy-evaluator results (not a safety certificate), open-loop, 5 s horizon, U.S. rules, validation split. Source code: [1]. 

Index Terms —autonomous driving, trajectory selection, rule compliance, lexicographic ranking, priority-aware reranking, multi-modal prediction, Waymo Open Motion Dataset, differ-entiable proxies, rule applicability prediction

We present RECTOR (Rule-Enforced Constrained Trajectory ORchestrator), a post-generation reranking layer that orders candidates under the priority Safety ≻ Legal ≻ Road ≻ Comfort using an ε-lexicographic rule. RECTOR has three pieces: a scene-conditioned applicability mechanism over 28 rules (retained as a tested reference component, not the deployment-default; see Section VI), 24 differentiable rule proxies for batched evaluation, and a deterministic tolerance-based lexicographic selector. The selector is interchangeable with any predictor that outputs world-frame candidates and confidences. The central claim is that proxy-evaluator compliance can be improved at selection time when the candidate set con-tains an acceptable option. Generation and selection are distinct responsibilities: the generator covers plausible fu-tures; the reranker picks the least-violating trajectory un-der explicit priorities, flags infeasibility when none is fully compliant, and avoids collapsing heterogeneous rules into a single penalty. A. Related Work and Positioning 

Most prior work improves the candidate set rather than the executed mode. Anchor-based predictors (LaneGCN, TNT/DenseTNT), Transformer set predictors (MTR, Way-former, QCNet, GameFormer), and latent-variable models (Trajectron++, MotionLM) target multimodal coverage [2]– [10]. Constraint-guided generators (CTG, CTG++, Motion-Diffuser) bias generation toward compliant regions [11]–[13]. These methods strengthen the proposal distribution but do not impose a priority hierarchy on the single trajectory that is executed. Constraints are also enforced earlier in the stack. Planning-and control-time methods such as MPC, control barrier func-tions, reachability analysis, and rulebook-based control embed constraints in online optimization [14]–[16]. Policy-time meth-ods such as constrained RL and SafetyNet internalize safety

during learning [17]–[19]. Both target different intervention points: RECTOR addresses post-generation reranking while leaving the predictor unchanged. The closest comparison is scalar reranking. Weighted penal-ties and differentiable optimization layers (OptNet, differ-entiable MPC) rank pre-generated candidates, but cross-tier behavior depends on weight calibration [20]–[22]. RSS and liability rulebooks motivate explicit priority structure [23], [24] but do not address learned scene-conditioned applicability over motion candidates. RECTOR targets post-generation selection on a fixed candidate set, predicts applicability from scene context, scores with smooth rule proxies, and enforces priority structurally rather than through tuned weights. Consistent with that focus, we evaluate selected-trajectory accuracy and rule compliance, not only candidate-set coverage. 

B. Contributions 

In the reference instantiation, a Transformer–CVAE gener-ator inspired by M2I produces K=6 candidates over a 5 s horizon ( T =50 at 10 Hz), evaluated against R=28 rules in four tiers (Safety: 5, Legal: 7, Road: 2, Comfort: 14); 24 are scored by differentiable proxies and 4 are audit-only. Candi-dates are ranked lexicographically, and confidence breaks ties that are compliance-equivalent; the selector is deterministic, parameter-free, and leaves the generator unchanged. The con-tributions are: 

C1 Rule-aware selection measurably improves proxy compliance on a fixed candidate set. Under Protocol B (28 rules, oracle applicability), rule-aware reranking reduces Safety+Legal violations from 28.58% to 20.42% (8.16 pp) and Total from 40.32% to 32.41% (7.91 pp) versus confidence-only on 43,219 instances. The de-ployed Protocol A (24 rules, learned head) shows larger apparent gains, but its inflated baseline reflects Safety over-activation and Legal/Road suppression by the head; we therefore lead with Protocol B. A uniform-weight weighted-sum baseline matches the lexicographic binary compliance on this benchmark, so the empirical lift is rule-aware over confidence-only. 

C2 ε-Lexicographic selection with a weight-free cross-tier guarantee. The selector preserves Safety ≻ Le-gal ≻ Road ≻ Comfort over the normalized tier scores within tolerance ε; no scalar reweighting can replicate this. The guarantee is structural and proxy-conditional, not a control-theoretic or jurisdictional safety certifi-cate. Lexicographic and weighted-sum match here only because cross-tier conflicts are rare in WOMD ( does not , not cannot ; Sec. VI). The selector also produces a regression-testable decision trace. 

C3 Structural robustness to upstream confidence mis-calibration. Under an adversarial protocol that injects a Safety-violating candidate with the maximum confi-dence, confidence-only selection fails in 100% of sce-narios; both rule-aware selectors reject the injection in 

∼96% across three injection families—structural robust-ness no scalar recalibration can match.

We use the Waymo Open Motion Dataset (WOMD) v1.3.0 [25], which provides temporally aligned trajectories for the ego and surrounding agents, HD map context (lane topology, crosswalks, boundaries), and traffic-control state (signals, stop signs) required for both motion prediction and rule applicability/violation computation. Table VI summarizes key statistics. Fig. 3 shows representative interactive scenarios spanning all four tiers. We follow WOMD’s official interactive splits to avoid leakage. Training uses 102,040 augmented interactive scenarios; the selector benchmark uses the augmented 

validation_interactive split (43,219 evaluation in-stances from 12,800 validation scenarios across 150 shards). Each augmented TFRecord wraps the original Scenario proto with per-rule applicability labels, violation flags, and severity scores from waymo_rule_eval ; these labels are the oracle ground truth used in ablations (Table XI). The test split is withheld. The ∼3.4 × evaluation -set augmentation (12,800 → 43,219) comes from ego-perspective permutation: each base scenario with two objects_of_interest is evaluated once per ego designation (including the original sdc_index ), giving 2–4 instances per base scenario depending on interaction type and validity-mask coverage. Higher absolute violation rates than single-perspective evaluation reflect harder interaction roles (yielding, merging) for which the predictor is less calibrated. WOMD defines an interactive scenario by exactly two objects_of_interest —the primary interacting entities—emphasizing cases where inter-agent coupling and priority reasoning are salient, an appropriate stress test for rule-aware selection. We use all interaction types in the validation split: v2v, v2p, v2c, and others. Filtering is minimal and removes only degenerate cases: scenarios with fewer than 2 agents, ego speed consistently below 0.1 m/s, or incomplete map features. About 98% of scenarios survive. The Legal-tier catalog reflects U.S. right-hand traffic norms in WOMD; other jurisdictions require re-parameterizing the catalog and re-evaluating proxy coverage.

1) Cannot vs. Does Not: Why WS = Lex on This Benchmark:

This section verifies the rule-evaluation and selection pipeline at the invariant, numerical, and integration levels, then stress-tests it under perturbations and edge cases. We verify four invariants required by tier aggregation: non-negativity of violation scores (100%), applicability dominance so inactive rules contribute zero (99.2% post-mitigation, with residual boundary-adjacent map cases logged for regression), temporal consistency of per-timestep aggregation (100%), and monotonicity of threshold-based rules (100%). This appendix documents the hypothesis tests, confidence interval procedures, and software references used in Sec-tions V and VI. 

A. McNemar’s Test (Paired Binary Compliance) 

McNemar’s test is applied to compare whether two selectors (e.g., lexicographic vs. confidence-only) differ significantly in binary rule compliance on the same set of scenarios. 

Setup. For each scenario i, let bi = ⊮[selector A violates ] and 

ci = ⊮[selector B violates ]. The discordant counts are B =P 

> i

bi(1 − ci) (A violates, B complies) and C = P 

> i

ci(1 − bi)

(B violates, A complies). 

Statistic. For B + C ≥ 25 , the continuity-corrected statistic is: 

χ2 = (|B − C| − 1) 2

B + C , (40) which is compared against χ21. For small B+C < 25 , the exact binomial test is used ( p-value = 2 · Binomial (B; B + C, 0.5) 

two-sided). 

Significance threshold. α = 0 .05 (two-sided). All reported p-values are uncorrected; with 28 rules the Bonferroni threshold is α′ = 0 .0018 .

B. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (Paired Continuous Metrics) 

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is applied to compare per-scenario selADE or tier scores between two selectors. It is preferred over the paired t-test because selADE distributions are heavy-tailed (Section II). 

Statistic. Let di = xAi − xBi be per-scenario differences. Ties (di = 0 ) are excluded; ranks are assigned to |di| and the signed-rank sum W + is computed. 

Significance threshold. α = 0 .05 (two-sided). 22 

C. Kolmogorov–Smirnov Test (Distribution Comparison) 

The two-sample KS test is used to compare the distribution of per-scenario compliance scores between evaluation subsets (e.g., validation vs. test split, or protocol-A vs. protocol-B). 

Statistic. D = sup x |F1(x) − F2(x)|, where F1, F 2 are empirical CDFs. 

D. Bootstrap Confidence Intervals 

Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals are reported for viola-tion rates and selADE. 

Procedure. 

1) Draw B=10 ,000 bootstrap resamples (with replacement) from the N per-scenario measurements. 2) Compute the statistic (e.g., violation rate) on each resam-ple. 3) Report the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution as the 95% CI. 

E. Spearman Rank Correlation 

Spearman ρ is used to measure rank correlation between proxy severity and full-evaluator severity across all K=6 

candidates per scenario (Table ?? ). 

Note on zero-variance rules. When one or both evaluators return zero variance (a constant value across all candidates), ρ

is undefined and reported as “—”. These rules achieve pairwise concordance = 1 .0 trivially and are excluded from the mean ρ

computation; the mean ρ=0 .25 is computed over the 10 rules with measurable variance in both evaluators.
