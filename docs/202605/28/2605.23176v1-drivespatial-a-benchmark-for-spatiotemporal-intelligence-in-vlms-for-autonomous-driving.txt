## Abstract 

Spatiotemporal intelligence in autonomous driving (AD) requires an agent to in-tegrate multi-view observations into a coherent scene representation, maintain

However, existing AD vision-language bench-marks largely focus on single-view, static, ego-centric, or single-source question answering, leaving it unclear whether current Vision-Language Models (VLMs) can truly construct and reason over dynamic driving scenes. a benchmark of 15.6K human-verified QA pairs across 20 tasks from five large-scale AD datasets. DRIVESPATIAL evaluates four abilities: Cognitive Scene Construction , Multi-view Relational Understanding , Temporal Reasoning , and

Unlike prior benchmarks, DRIVESPATIAL is generated from a dynamic multi-relational scene graph that encodes object states, spatial relations, interactions, camera visibility, and temporal correspondences, enabling QA pairs that enforce genuine cross-view and spatiotemporal reasoning. Evaluating 15 repre-sentative VLMs reveals a substantial human–model gap: the strongest model trails humans by 28.4 points, with Cognitive Scene Construction emerging as the key bottleneck. Further diagnostics show that language-only prompting is insufficient, while explicit BEV grounding consistently improves performance. These results suggest that current VLMs lack the scene-construction ability needed for reliable spatiotemporal driving intelligence. DRIVESPATIAL and its construction pipeline will be released to support future research. 

## 1 Introduction 

Spatiotemporal Intelligence refers to the ability to mentally construct, understand, and reason about an internal representation from the surrounding environment. It is a fundamental component of human cognition for navigation, particularly in driving scenarios [ 1, 2, 3, 4]. By integrating information from multiple viewpoints, humans form a coherent spatial representation that supports relational reasoning, occlusion understanding, and risk anticipation. As vision–language models (VLMs) become increasingly embedded in Autonomous Driving (AD) [ 5, 6, 7, 8 , 9 , 10 , 11 , 12 ], evaluating models with comparable spatial reasoning capabilities becomes essential for robust perception, interaction, and decision-making. Recent VLM-based AD systems and benchmarks have made substantial progress in language-grounded perception, prediction, and planning [ 13 , 6, 14 , 5, 12 , 15 , 16 , 17 ]. However, current evaluations still leave a critical question unresolved: do these models truly understand the spatial structure and temporal dynamics of a driving scene, or do they mostly rely on local recognition cues and dataset-specific regularities? Most existing benchmarks rely heavily on a single dataset such as nuScenes [ 18 ], focus on static scenes [ 19 , 18 , 20 , 21 , 7, 22 , 23 , 24 ], restrict reasoning to a single camera view [ 23 , 19 , 21 , 18 , 22 , 15 , 24 ], or offer limited question diversity [ 20 , 25 ]. As a result, strong benchmark performance may overestimate a model’s true scene-level reasoning ability. This gap is clearer when comparing what current benchmarks actually test. nuScenes-QA and related driving QA datasets remain largely ego-centric and do not explicitly enforce cross-view integration [ 26 , 18 ]. DriveLM and OmniDrive broaden driving-oriented QA, but their evaluations are still centered on perception, prediction, and planning rather than testing whether a model can reconstruct a coherent scene from synchronized camera streams [ 15 , 5]. Ego3D-Bench and STSBench move closer to spatial and spatiotemporal evaluation, yet they still do not jointly probe scene construction, cross-view identity and relation reasoning, and temporal interaction reasoning under a single benchmark [ 20 , 25 ]. In addition, many existing questions can still be solved as conventional 2D visual question answering [ 21 , 7, 5 , 18 ], without requiring a coherent multi-view scene representation. We argue that this mismatch comes from a gap between current benchmark design and real driving cognition. Safe driving requires building a coherent scene representation from multiple viewpoints and time steps, using that representation to interpret relations and interactions, and maintaining those abilities across new sources and conditions. To fill these gaps, we introduce DRIVE SPATIAL , a benchmark for evaluating spatiotemporal intelli-gence in AD. D RIVE SPATIAL is derived from five large-scale AD benchmarks [ 27 , 28 , 29 , 30 , 31 ]and contains 15.6k human-verified question-answer pairs spanning 20 tasks. While prior benchmarks rely on multi-view images alone and leave cross-view reasoning implicit, D RIVE SPATIAL constructs its questions from a dynamic multi-relational scene graph that encodes object states, spatial relations, interactions, actions, camera visibility, and temporal correspondences across frames. The benchmark is organized around three core reasoning abilities and one robustness axis: (1) Cognitive Scene Construction ( Const. ), the ability to build an internal representation of the surrounding environment from multi-view observations; (2) Multi-view Relational Understanding ( Unders. ), the ability to interpret spatial relationships among objects across multiple viewpoints; (3) Temporal Reasoning 

( Reas. ), the ability to connect observations over time to infer causal dynamics and anticipate future events; and (4) Generalization ( Gen. ), which measures whether the first three abilities remain reliable across diverse driving scenarios and data sources. Table 1 provides a comparison between DRIVE SPATIAL and the existing multi-view QA benchmarks for AD. Our contributions are: (i) We introduce D RIVE SPATIAL , a spatiotemporal intelligence benchmark comprising 15.6k QA pairs across 20 tasks, curated from five AD datasets and spanning diverse weather, scene, and lighting conditions. (ii) We propose a scalable benchmark construction pipeline 

that combines a dynamic multi-relational scene graph with structured human verification to enforce cross-view and temporal constraints during QA generation. (iii) We conduct an extensive evaluation 

of 15 representative VLMs and show a 28.4-point human-model gap on D RIVE SPATIAL -mini , with Const. emerging as the dominant bottleneck. We further find that language-only prompting does not close this gap, while explicit spatial grounding consistently improves performance.

Spatial and Spatiotemporal Intelligence in VLMs. A growing body of work probes whether VLMs possess genuine spatial intelligence. General-purpose benchmarks evaluate static spatial relations [ 49 ,50 , 51 ], grounded and 3D-aware reasoning [ 52 , 53 , 54 , 55 , 56 , 57 , 58 ], and broader capability suites [ 59 , 60 , 61 , 62 , 63 ]. Video-based benchmarks extend this to spatiotemporal reasoning [ 64 ,65 , 66 , 67 , 68 ], and recent studies highlight specific failure modes such as egocentric bias and weak perspective-taking [ 69 , 70 , 71 , 72 ], occlusion-aware reasoning [ 73 , 74 ], and difficulty in maintaining cognitive maps from video [ 75 , 76 , 77 ]. D RIVE SPATIAL complements these efforts by grounding spatiotemporal evaluation specifically in multi-view, multi-source driving scenes. 

Benchmarks for Spatial Understanding in AD. Driving QA benchmarks span scene description, perception and prediction, driving advice, attention alignment, and counterfactual reasoning [ 78 , 20 ,19 , 18 , 21 , 5, 7 , 15 , 22 , 24 , 23 , 79 , 26 , 80 , 81 , 82 ], with several leveraging structured scene-graph representations of road actors and their relations [ 83 , 84 ]. Despite this progress, most are limited by single-source data, static observations, single-view reasoning, or question forms answerable from local visual cues alone. D RIVE SPATIAL complements rather than replaces these benchmarks: it spans multiple datasets and ego platforms, explicitly enforces cross-view and temporal constraints during question generation, and unifies scene construction, multi-view relational reasoning, and temporal reasoning under one taxonomy, making it a better fit for diagnosing whether a VLM can build and use a coherent scene-level representation in dynamic driving environments.

3.1 Benchmark Construction Pipeline 

DRIVE SPATIAL construction pipeline includes four stages, as in Figure 4. Human-in-the-loop verification is applied at both the metadata and QA generation stages to ensure annotation quality. Full details are provided in Appendix A. 

Data Collection and Calibration. We standardize five public AD validation sets [ 30 , 27 , 28 , 29 , 31 ]into a unified interface. Because these datasets do not share the same coordinate convention, we adopt nuScenes as the reference system and align all other datasets. 

Metadata Completion. We complete missing scene-level annotations required for graph-based QA generation and generalization analysis. We infer weather and time of day via SigLIP-2 [ 85 ]image–text similarity on sampled keyframes, and classify scene type by querying Qwen3-VL-8B [ 86 ]on rendered top-down maps. All inferred labels are then verified by domain experts.

In total, this verification stage required approximately 160 annotator-hours, with 76.86% of generated questions passing all quality criteria; the remainder were either revised or discarded. 

## 4 Benchmark Evaluation 

4.1 Evaluation Setup Benchmark models. We evaluate 15 VLMs across diverse model families, scales, and training paradigms, grouped into three categories: proprietary models ( Proprietary ) [ 87 , 88 , 89 ], general-purpose open-source models ( Generalists ), including both image-based [ 39 , 40 , 90 , 91 , 37 , 34 ] and video-based architectures [ 92 , 93 ], and domain-specialized models ( Specialists ) for autonomous driving and spatial reasoning [10, 20] or 3D embodied tasks [94, 95]. 

Evaluation Tasks and Metrics. For multiple-choice answer (MCA) tasks, we follow prior MCA benchmarks [ 96 , 97 , 98 , 99 ] and report exact-match accuracy. For open-ended tasks requiring absolute numeric predictions (e.g., distances, counts, or spatial measurements), we report the root mean squared error (RMSE) against ground truth. Additionally, we report Cohen’s κ agreement [ 100 ]to assess the alignment between human annotators and VLM predictions. 

Baselines. To contextualize model performance, we establish three baselines: two chance-level baselines including Random (uniform answer selection) and Frequency (the most frequent answer), and a Human-Level on D RIVE SPATIAL -mini , a stratified subset of 1,000 questions (50 per task). Human annotators answer each question independently. Full implementation details, including frame sampling, camera ordering, context limits, and prompt templates, are provided in Appendix B.

Diagnostic Studies A. Impact of Linguistic Prompting. We first examine whether standard prompting strategies improve performance on D RIVE SPATIAL . Following prior work [ 101 , 102 , 103 , 99 , 104 ], we evaluate VLMs 8from distinct families: Qwen3-VL, Gemma-3 and Ego3D. Specifically, we consider Chain-of-Thought 

(CoT) [ 105 , 106 ], Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) [ 107 ], and Self-Consistency (SC) [ 108 ]. As shown in Table 4 (Left) (Left), none of these methods improves over the zero-shot (ZS) baseline under our evaluation setup; instead, all consistently degrade performance across the three models. B. Impact of Geometric Grounding. We further examine whether an explicit spatial prior can compensate for the missing geometric prior. We provide each model with a pre-constructed BEV map rendered from source annotations: HD-map-backed top-down layouts when available, and LiDAR-ground-plane projections overlaid with annotated 3D boxes otherwise. Because this input directly provides scene layout information, we exclude the Const. tasks from this ablation and treat the BEV map as a diagnostic spatial prior rather than a deployable perception module. As shown in Table 4 (Middle), BEV grounding consistently improves performance across all evaluated models, increasing average accuracy by 8.27% for Qwen3-VL, 4.95% for Gemma-3, and 4.55% for Ego3D. 

C. Impact of the Number of Views. To examine whether QAs in D RIVE SPATIAL require cross-view reasoning, we evaluate each model under 0-/2-/4-/full-view . The 0-view provides only the question and answer choices, measuring language priors without visual evidence, while 2-view and 4-view 

provide randomly sampled partial observations. As shown in Table 4 (Right), performance improves monotonically with more views, validating the cross-view constraints enforced during QA generation. 

## 5 Conclusion & Discussion 

We introduced DRIVE SPATIAL , a spatiotemporal intelligence benchmark for evaluating whether VLMs can construct coherent multi-view scene representations, reason about spatial relationships across viewpoints, infer temporal dynamics, and remain reliable across diverse driving conditions. Built from five large-scale AD datasets, D RIVE SPATIAL contains 15.6k human-verified QA pairs spanning 20 tasks organized around Const. , Unders. , Reas. , and Gen. . Our evaluation of 15 representative VLMs reveals a substantial human-model gap, with Const. emerging as the clearest bottleneck. Our diagnostic studies further show that linguistic prompting does not close this gap, while explicit spatial grounding through BEV representations consistently improves performance. 

Discussion: These findings suggest several directions for future research. First, improving scene construction through stronger 3D-aware representation learning and multi-view fusion may help mod-els build more reliable internal spatial representations. Second, integrating complementary sensing modalities, such as LiDAR or map priors, may improve robustness in visually degraded environments. Finally, the limited gains from linguistic-only prompting indicate a need for architectures and training paradigms that explicitly model spatial structure and temporal dynamics. 

Impact: By exposing the limitations of current VLMs, D RIVE SPATIAL provides diagnostic tools that may support the development of safer and more reliable AI systems for autonomous driving. Beyond AD, the benchmark can also benefit robotics, embodied AI, and multimodal reasoning, where agents must understand spatial relationships across viewpoints and time. 9References

A.6 Verification Tools & Insight A.6.1 Verification Tools 

To enable fast and efficient verification of our AD dataset, we developed an interactive tool that integrates camera views, LiDAR visualization, annotations, dynamic multi-relational graph visual-ization, metadata inspection, and question generation. The system is implemented using Next.js for the frontend and FastAPI for the backend, with web-based system, it is easy to access for everyone. Figure A-V illustrates the metadata verification interface, where users can quickly scan through frames within a scene and modify scene-level metadata by clicking the “Edit” button. In addition, the interface supports verification of the dynamic multi-relational graphs, including spatial graph verification (Figure A-VI), action graph verification (Figure A-VII), and interaction graph verification (Figure A-VIII). For question verification, we use a separate UI that asks annotators to review each question and reject those considered incorrect based on four criteria: (1) correctness of the answer, (2) uniqueness of the correct option, (3) plausibility of the question, and (4) visibility of the referenced objects. The interface used for this verification stage is shown in Figure A-IX. 

A.6.2 Verification Insight 

As shown in Figure A-X, the average time annotators spent on metadata verification is approximately 181.47 seconds per scene. The maximum time reaches 3854.76 seconds, which occurs in the ONCE dataset. Although ONCE contains only four scenes, each scene includes around 2000 frames, leading to significantly longer verification time. In contrast, annotators spent the largest portion of the total verification time on the Argoverse 2 benchmark due to its substantially larger scale (23,547 frames). For question verification, as illustrated in Figure A-XI, annotators spent a total of 80.24 hours reviewing the generated questions. The majority of this time was spent on reasoning tasks, which involve temporal reasoning and more complex scene analysis, with an average verification time of 139.27 seconds per question. In comparison, construction and understanding tasks require significantly less time, averaging 8.25 seconds and 5.03 seconds per question, respectively. 41 Metadata Editing

C.3 Limitation Discussion 

While D RIVE SPATIAL advances the evaluation of spatiotemporal intelligence in AD, several limi-tations remain. First, our benchmark is constructed from five existing large-scale AD datasets, and although this provides broad coverage of geographies, sensor configurations, and driving conditions, it inherits the distributional biases of those source datasets; incorporating additional data sources such as simulation may be needed to cover long-tail scenarios, and these can be seamlessly integrated into our pipeline. Second, our evaluation focuses on multiple-choice QA, which provides a controlled and reproducible measurement protocol but cannot fully capture open-ended reasoning, planning, or inter-active decision-making behaviors that real driving requires; extending the benchmark to free-form generation and closed-loop evaluation is left to future work. Finally, we evaluate 15 representative VLMs spanning open- and closed-source families, but the rapid pace of model development means our leaderboard reflects a snapshot rather than a stable ranking, and the relative ordering of models may shift as new architectures, training recipes, and spatial-grounding techniques emerge. 47 NeurIPS Paper Checklist
